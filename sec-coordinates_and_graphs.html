    Section 4.2 Coordinates and Graphs

    \(\textbf{Coordinates}\) refer to an ordered pair of numbers (x, y), that represent the exact location of a point on the Cartesian plane.
    • The \(\textbf{x-coordinate}\) tells you how far left or right the point is from the origin.
    • The \(\textbf{y-coordinate}\) tells you how far up or down the point is from the origin.

    Subsection 4.2.1 Cartesian Plane

    Activity 4.2.1.

    \({\color{black} \textbf{Work in groups}}\)
    1. Draw Two Perpendicular Lines
      • Use a ruler to draw a horizontal line (x-axis).
      • Draw a vertical line (y-axis) that intersects the x-axis at a right angle.
      • Label the intersection point as (0,0), called the origin.
    2. Label the Axes
      • Write “x-axis” below the horizontal line.
      • Write “y-axis” beside the vertical line.
    3. Mark Equal Intervals
      • Choose a scale (e.g., 1, 2, 5, or 10).
      • Mark and label positive numbers to the right on the x-axis and above on the y-axis.
      • Mark and label negative numbers to the left on the x-axis and below on the y-axis.
    4. Check
      • Ensure the numbers are evenly spaced.
      • Make sure the x-axis and y-axis are clearly labeled.
    5. Discuss and compare your diagrams
    \({\color{black} \textbf{Key Takeaway}}\)
    1. X-Axis is the horizontal number line in the Cartesian plane.
    2. Y-Axis is the vertical number line in the Cartesian plane.
    The Cartesian plane is a two-dimensional coordinate system defined by two perpendicular number lines
    • The \(\textbf{x-axis}\text{,}\) (horizontal line).
    • The \(\textbf{y-axis}\text{,}\) (vertical line).
    These axes intersect at a point called the \(\textbf{origin (0,0)}\) , dividing the plane into four.

    \({\color{black} \textbf{Key Takeaway}}\)
    • Coordinates represents a pair of numbers written as \((x, y)\) that represents a point’s location on the Cartesian plane.
    • Origin is the point where the x-axis and y-axis intersect, denoted as \((0,0)\text{.}\)

    Example 4.2.2.

    A school wants to create a mini park on its playground. The park will have four trees planted at specific locations, forming a rectangular area. The positions of the trees are given as:
    A \((2,3)\text{,}\)B \((2,7)\text{,}\)C \((6,7)\text{,}\)D \((6,3)\)
    1. Plot these points on a Cartesian plane.
    2. Identify the shape formed by connecting the points.
    3. Find the length and width of the rectangle.
    Solution.
    2. Connecting the points in order forms a rectangle.
    3. Finding Length and Width:
      • Distance between A and B or C and D (same x-coordinates) = \(\textbf{7 - 3 = 4}\textbf{units (height)}\)
      • Distance between A and D or B and C (same y-coordinates) = \(\textbf{6 - 2 = 4}\textbf{units (width)}\)
      The dimensions of the rectangle are 4 × 4 units (a square).

    Subsection 4.2.4 Table of Values for Linear Equations

    Activity 4.2.4.

    \({\color{black} \textbf{Work in groups}}\)
    Consider the linear equation \(\textbf{y = 2x + 1}\)
    Create a table of values by choosing different \(\textbf{x-values}\) and calculating \(y\text{.}\)
    Discuss and share the results with other groups
    \({\color{black} \textbf{Key Takeaway}}\)
    A linear equation is usually in the form \(\textbf{y = mx + b}\)
    where
    • \(m\) is the slope (rate of change).
    • \(b\) is the y-intercept (where the line crosses the y-axis).
    A table of values helps us organize points that satisfy a linear equation.
    By substituting different values of x, we calculate corresponding y-values to create a set of ordered pairs \((x,y)\)
    These points can then be plotted on a Cartesian plane to form a straight line.

    Example 4.2.3.

    Complete the table of values for the linear equation:
    \(\textbf{y = -x + 4}\)
    for example,\(x\) = \(2\) then
    \(\textbf{y = -2 + 4}\)
    = \(2\)
    x -2 -1 0 1 2
    y ? ? ? ? \(2\)
    Solution.
    x Calculation y Value
    -2 \(\textbf{y = −(−2) + 4 = 2 + 4}\) \(6\)
    -1 \(\textbf{y = −(−1) + 4 = 1 + 4}\) \(5\)
    0 \(\textbf{y = −(0) + 4 = 0 + 4}\) \(4\)
    1 \(\textbf{y = −(1) + 4 = −1 + 4}\) \(3\)
    2 \(\textbf{y = −(2) + 4 = −2 + 4}\) \(2\)

    Example 4.2.4.

    consider the Linear equation \(\textbf{y} = -\frac{\textbf{1}}{\textbf{2}}x + \textbf{4}\)
    1. From the Linear equation, fill the following table
      \(x\) \(-4\) \(-2\) \(0\) \(2\) \(4\)
      \(y\) ? ? \(4\) ? ?
    2. From the table,plot the points for corresponding values of x and y on a Cartesian plane.
    3. Using a straight line join the points
    Solution.
    1. The complete table is
      \(x\) \(Calculation\) \(\textbf{y Value}\)
      -4 \(\textbf{y} = -\frac{\textbf{1}}{\textbf{2}}(-4) + \textbf{4} = 2 + 4 =\textbf{6}\) \(6\)
      -2 \(\textbf{y} = -\frac{\textbf{1}}{\textbf{2}}(-2) + \textbf{4} = 1 + 4 =\textbf{5}\) \(5\)
      0 \(\textbf{y} = -\frac{\textbf{1}}{\textbf{2}}(0) + \textbf{4} = 0 + 4 =\textbf{4}\) \(4\)
      2 \(\textbf{y} = -\frac{\textbf{1}}{\textbf{2}}(2) + \textbf{4} = -1 + 4 =\textbf{3}\) \(3\)
      4 \(\textbf{y} = -\frac{\textbf{1}}{\textbf{2}}(4) + \textbf{4} = -2 + 4 =\textbf{2}\) \(2\)

    Activity 4.2.6.

    \({\color{black} \textbf{Work in groups}}\)
    Materials Needed
    • Graph Paper
    • Ruler

    2. Create a Table of Values

    \({\color{black} \textbf{Key Takeaway}}\)
    The general form of a linear equation:\(y=mx+c \)

    Example 4.2.5.

    Consider the equation:
    \(\textbf{y} = -\frac{1}{2}\textbf{x} + \textbf{3}\)
    1. Create a Table of Values
    2. Plot the Points
    3. Connect the points with a straight line extending in both directions.
    Solution.
    1. Creating a Table of Values:
      \(x\) \(y \, = \, -\frac{1}{2}\textbf{x} + \textbf{3}\)
      \(-4\) \(y \, = \, -\frac{1}{2} \, \times \, -4 + \textbf{3} \, = \, 2+3 =5\) \(5\)
      \(-2\) \(y \, = \, -\frac{1}{2}\, \times \,-2 + 3 \, = \, 1+3 =4\) \(4\)
      \(0\) \(y = -\frac{1}{2}\, \times \,0 + 3 \, = \,0+3 =3\) \(3\)
      \(2\) \(y = -\frac{1}{2}\, \times \,2 + 3 \, = \, -1 + 3 = 2\) \(2\)
      \(4\) \(y = -\frac{1}{2}\, \times \,4 + 3 \, = \, -2 + 3 = 1\) \(1\)
    2. Plot the Points
      • \(\displaystyle (-4,5)\)
      • \(\displaystyle (-2,4)\)
      • \(\displaystyle (0,3)\)
      • \(\displaystyle (2,2)\)
      • \(\displaystyle (4,1)\)
    \({\color{black} \textbf{Take notice}}\)
    1. The slope is \(-\frac{1}{2}\text{,}\) meaning the line slopes downward
    2. The y-intercept is \(3\text{,}\) where the line crosses the y-axis.

    Subsection 4.2.7 Solving Linear Simultaneous Equations Using Graphs

    Linear simultaneous equations are algebraic equations with the same variables and are solved at the same time.
    One of the ways of solving simultaneous equations is graphical method.

    Activity 4.2.7.

    Materials Needed
    • Graph paper
    • Rulers
    • Pencils
    • Example equations
    Instructions
    Solve the following linear simultaneous equations graphically.
    \begin{equation*} y = 2x + 6 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} y = -x + 3 \end{equation*}
    1. Create a table for the \(x \text{ and } y\) coordinates of each equation. Choose \(5\) values of \(x\) to calculate the \(y\) coordinates for the two tables.
    2. Plot the points for each equation on the same graph paper. Use a ruler to draw straight lines through the points for each equation.
    3. Locate the point where the two lines intersect on their graph and write down the coordinates.
    4. Share your results with your fellow learners.

    Example 4.2.6.

    Solve the following simultaneous equations graphically:
    \begin{equation*} y = -x + 4 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} y = 2x +1 \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    Make separate tables of \(x \text{ and } y\) variables for each equation.
    Construct the two lines for the equations in your graph as shown below.
    Identify the point of intersection of the two lines, that is point \((1,3)\)
    The solution for the two simultaneous equations is the coordinates of the intersection of the two lines. \(x = 1\) and \(y = 3.\)

    Subsection 4.2.8 Application of Linear Simultaneous Equations in Real Life

    Simultaneous equations are used in solving word problem questions with unknown variables. Algebraic equations are formed from the word problem.
    One of the real life applications of simultaneous equations in real life is in business to determine costs of items.

    Activity 4.2.8.

    Materials needed
    • Graph paper
    • Ruler and writing materials
    Juma, a vendor in Gikomba market, sells bananas and mangoes. One of his customers, Aisha, bought \(5\) mangoes and \(2\) bananas at a cost of \(Ksh.\, 300.\) Another customer, Kinara, bought \(3\) mangoes and \(4\) bananas for \(Ksh. \, 240.\) Find the price of a mango and a banana.
    Instructions
    1. Form two simultaneous equations from the information above. Use \(Ksh.\, m\) as the cost of a mango and \(Ksh.\, b\) as the cost of a banana.
    2. Create two tables of value for the coordinates of the two simultaneous equations.
    3. Construct the two lines for the two equations in your graph paper.
    4. Locate the point where the two lines intersect on their graph and write down the coordinates.
    5. What is the cost of a mango and a banana?
    6. Discuss your findings with your fellow learners.

    Example 4.2.10.

    Mara and Abdi went to the shop to buy books and pens. Mara bought two books and one pen at a cost of \(120\) shillings. Abdi bought two books and two pens at a cost of \(140\) shillings. Find the cost of a book and a pen. Use \(x\) to represent the cost of a book and use \(y\) to represent the cost of a pen.
    Solution.
    Cost of a book: \(x\)
    Cost of a pen: \(y\)
    Amount of money Mara spent on buying \(2\) books is: \(2 \times x = 2x\)
    The cost of buying \(1\) pen is: \(1 \times y = y\)
    The total cost of buying \(2\) books and \(1\) pen is:
    \begin{equation*} 2x + y = 120 \end{equation*}
    Amount of money Abdi spent on buying \(2\) books is: \(2 \times x = 2x\)
    The cost of buying \(2\) pens is: \(2 \times y = 2y\)
    The sum of buying \(2\) books and \(2\) pens is:
    \begin{equation*} 2x + 2y = 140 \end{equation*}
    The two simultaneous equations formed are:
    \begin{equation*} 2x + y = 120. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 2x + 2y = 140. \end{equation*}
    Form two tables from the two simultaneous equations
    Construct the two lines for the equations in your graph as shown below.
    The solution for the two simultaneous equations is the coordinates of the intersection of the two lines.
    \(x = 50\) and \(y = 20.\)
    Therefore:
    Cost of a book is \(50.\)
    Cost of a pen is \(20.\)
