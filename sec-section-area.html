    Section 3.2 Area

    Area is the space covered or enclosed within the boundaries of a shape.
    We calculate the area of different shapes such as triangles, rectangles, squares, circles, and many others.

    Subsection 3.2.1 Area of a circle

    It is calculated using the formula \(\textbf{A} = \pi r^2 \) where \(\pi = \frac{22}{7}\) or \(3.142\) and \(r \,\text{is the radius of the circle} \text{.}\) It is measured in square units.

    Subsubsection 3.2.1.1 Calculating the area of a circle in different situations.

    Activity 3.2.1.
    \(\textbf{Work in groups}\)
    What you need:
    A ruler, pieces of strings and pieces of sticks or chalks.
    1. On the ground, mark a point using a piece of stick and lable it O.
    2. Tie a stick with each end of the string and fix one stick on the ground at point O.
      1. Stretch the string and draw a circle as shown on the right.
      2. Draw several other circles using different lengths of strings.
      1. Measure the radius of each circle that you have drawn.
      2. Using formula, \(\textbf{area}= \pi r^2\text{,}\) calculate the area of each circle.
    5. Draw circles in your exersise book and work out the area of each.
    6. Share your work with other learners in your class.
    \(\textbf{Key Takeaway:}\)
    Area of a circle of radius \((r)\) is given by:
    \begin{equation*} A=\pi r^2 \end{equation*}
    Example 3.2.1.
    Find the area of a circle whose area is \(14 \, \text{cm}\) use \(\pi = \frac{22}{7}\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    \(\textbf{Area of a circle}= \pi r^2\)
    \begin{align*} \text{Area} =\amp \frac{22}{7} \times 14 \times 14 \\ = \amp 616 \, cm^2 \end{align*}
    Example 3.2.2.
    Find the area of each of the following circles with \(O\) as the centre use \(\pi = \frac{22}{7}\text{:}\)
    Solution.
    \(\textbf{(a)}\) \(\text{Area} = \pi r^2\)
    We are given;
    \begin{align*} \text{Diametter} =\amp 42 \,cm\\ \text{Radius(r)}= \amp \frac{42}{2} \\ = \amp 21 \,cm \end{align*}
    Therefore;
    \begin{align*} Area= \amp \frac{22}{7} \times 21 \times 21 \\ = \amp \frac{22}{7} \times 21^2 \\ = \amp 1\,386 \,cm^2 \end{align*}
    The area of \(\textbf{(a)}= {\color{blue}1\,386 \,cm^2}\)
    \(\textbf{(b)}\) \(\text{Area} = \pi r^2\)
    We are given;
    \(\text{Radius(r)}= 3.5\,cm\)
    Therefore;
    \begin{align*} \text{Area}=\amp \frac{22}{7} \times 3.5 \times 3.5 \\ =\amp \frac{22}{7} \times 3.5^2\\ =\amp 38.5 \, cm^2 \end{align*}
    The area of \(\textbf{(b)}= {\color{blue}38.5 \, cm^2}.\)

    Subsection 3.2.2 Area of a sector

    Subsubsection 3.2.2.1 Area of a sector of a circle(1)

    Activity 3.2.2.

    What you need:
    A graph paper and a razorblade or a parir of scissor
    1. Draw a circle of radius \(7 \, cm\) on a graph paper.
    2. Cut out the circle along its boundary.
    3. Mark the centre of the circle.
    4. Measure an angle of \(30^\circ\) at the centre and cut out as shown.
    5. Estimate the area by counting the number of squares enclosed by the arc and the two radii of the circle.
    6. Express the angle of the sector (\(30^\circ\)) as a fraction of the angle at the centre of the circle (\(360^\circ\)).
    7. Multiply the fraction obtained in (6) by the area of the circle.
    8. Discuss and share the result with other groups.
    \({\color{black}\textbf{Key Takeaway:}}\)
    A \(\textbf{sector}\) is a region bounded by two radii and an arc. A \(\textbf{minor}\) sector is one whose area is less than half the area of the circle while \(\textbf{major}\) sector is one whose area is greater than a half of the area of the circle, as shown below:
    \({\color{black}\textbf{The area of a Sector}}\)
    Consider the area of the shadedregion below;
    • The area of the whole circle is \(\pi r^2\text{.}\) The whole circle substends \(360^\circ\) at the centre.
      Therefore, \(360^\circ\) corresponds to \(\pi r^2\)
      \(1^\circ\) corresponds to \(\frac{1}{360} \times \pi r^2\)
      \(60^\circ\) corresponds to \(\frac{1}{360} \times \pi r^2\)
    \(\textbf{In general;}\) the area of a sector whose arc substends an angle at the centntre of the circle is given by;
    \begin{equation*} A =\frac{ \theta}{360}\times \pi \times r^2 \end{equation*}
    Example 3.2.3.
    Find the area of a sector of a circle of radius \(7\, cm\) if the angle substended at the centre is \(120^\circ\text{.}\) Write the answer to \(\textbf{2 decimal places}\text{.}\)
    Hint.
    Scetch the sector and use the formula below:
    \begin{equation*} A=\frac{\theta}{360} \times \pi r^2 \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    \(A=\frac{\theta}{360} \times \pi r^2 \)
    \(r=7 \,cm\) and \(\theta = 120^\circ\)
    Therefore;
    \begin{align*} A=\amp \frac{120}{360} \times \frac{22}{7} \times 7 \times 7 \\ =\amp \frac{120}{360} \times 22 \times 7 \\ =\amp 51.333333333 \,cm^2 \end{align*}
    In \(\textbf{2 decimal places}\) we have \(A= {\color{blue} 51.33\,cm^2}\text{.}\)

    Solution.
    \(\textbf{(a)}\) \(Area=\frac{\theta}{360^\circ} \pi r^2\)
    \begin{align*} A=\amp \frac{48^\circ}{360^\circ} \times \frac{22}{7} \times (4\,cm^2)\\ =\amp \frac{48^\circ}{360^\circ} \times \frac{22}{7} \times 4\,cm \times 4\,cm\\ = \amp 6.7047619 \,cm^2 \end{align*}

    Subsection 3.2.4 Surface area of cuboids

    In grade 7, you calculated the area of a squre and the area of a rectangle.
    In this section will look at the surface area of a cuboid.

    Subsubsection 3.2.4.1 Working out the surface area of cuboids in real life.

    Activity 3.2.5.
    \(\textbf{Work in groups}\)
    What you require: A model of a cuboid and a ruler.
    1. Get a cuboid like the one shown bellow.
    2. Identify the faces of the cuboid.
    3. What is the shape of each face of the cuboid.
    4. Measure the length of of all its sides.
    5. Identify three pairs of faces with equal dimensions.
    6. Calculate the area of each pair.
    7. What is the total area of all the faces of the cuboid?
    8. Discuss how to calculate the surface area of the cuboid and compare result with other groups.
    \({\color{black} \textbf{Key Takeaway:}}\)
    A \(\textbf{cuboid}\) is a three-dimensional figure or solid which has six rectangular sides called faces.
    It has 8 vertices and 12 edges. The opposite faces of a cuboid are always equal. It means that the opposite surfaces of the cuboid are in the same dimension.
    \(\textbf{Surface Area of Cuboid Formula}\)
    1. Before going into the concept of area, let us denote the dimensions of a cuboid, which are,
      Length, Width, and Height are represented by \(l\, ,w\, , \, \text{and}\,h\) respectively.
    2. The Total surface area of a cuboid (TSA) is equal to the sum of the areas of its \(6\) rectangular faces, which is given by:
      \begin{equation*} \textbf{Total Surface Area of a Cuboid (TSA)} {\color{blue} =2 (lw + wh + lh)} \,\textbf{square units}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      The above formula gives the total surface area of a cuboid having all six faces.
    \(\textbf{Note:}\) If incase one face is open, \(\textbf{DO NOT}\) multiply by two use it singly as, \(l \times w\) then add to the other faces.
    Example 3.2.10.
    Calculate the surface area of the cuboid below.
    Solution.
    Given that,
    \(l=15\,cm \quad w=8\,cm \quad \text{and} \quad h=10\,cm\)
    \begin{align*} \text{Surface area }= \amp 2(lw+wh+lh) \\ =\amp 2((15 \times 8)+(8 \times 10)+ (15 \times 10))\,cm^2\\ =\amp 2(120+80+150)\,cm^2\\ =\amp 2 \times 420\,cm^2\\ = \amp 700\,cm^2 \end{align*}
    Therefore, surface area \(={\color{blue} 700\,cm^2 }\)
    Example 3.2.11.
    Calculate the external surface area of the cuboid below opened at the top.
    Solution.
    Given that, \(l=12\,cm \quad w=9\,cm \quad \text{and} \quad h=7\,cm\)
    Since the top face ao the cuboid is open, the
    \begin{align*} \text{Surface area}=\amp lw+2(wh+lh) \\ = \amp 12 \times 9 + 2((9 \times 7)+ (12 \times 7))\\ = \amp 108\,cm^2+2(63+84)\\ = \amp 108\,cm^2+2(147)\\ = \amp 108\,cm^2+294\,cm^2\\ = \amp 402\,cm^2 \end{align*}
    Therefore, the external surface area of an open cuboid is \({\color{blue} 402\,cm^2 }\)
    Example 3.2.12.
    Identify the surface area of the block shown below.
    Solution.
    Given that \(l=5\,m \quad w=7\,m \quad \text{and} \quad h=18\,m\) .
    \begin{align*} \text{Surface area }= \amp 2(lw+wh+lh) \\ = \amp 2((5 \times 7)+(7 \times 18)+(5 \times 18) )\,m^2 \\ = \amp 2(35+126+40) \,m^2 \\ = \amp 2(201)\,m^2 \\ = \amp 402 \,m^2 \end{align*}
    The surface area of the block is \({\color{blue}402 \,m^2 }\)

    \({\color{Black} \textbf{Key Takaway:}}\)
    A \(\textbf{cylinder}\) is a three-dimensional structure having circular bases which are parallel to each other.
    A cylinder contains \(r \,\text{and}\, h\) where \(r\) represent the radius of the cylinder and \(h\) represent the height of the cylinder as shown below;
    \(\textbf{Area of a Cylinder Formula}\)
    1. The total area of a cylinder is based on:
      1. Curved Surface Area (CSA)
      2. Base Area
    \(\textbf{Curved Surface Area}\)
    The curved surface area of a cylinder (CSA) is defined as the area of the curved surface of any given cylinder having a base radius \(r\), and height \(h\)
    The formula for a curved area is given by:
    \begin{equation*} \textbf{CSA} = 2\pi \times r \times h \text{Square units} \end{equation*}
    \(\textbf{Base Area of Cylinder}\)
    The base of the cylinder is a circular shape. Hence, by the formula of area of the circle, we know,
    Area of the circular bases of cylinder \(= 2 (\pi r^2)\) [Since the cylinder has two circular bases]
    \(\textbf{Total Surface Area of Cylinder}\)
    \begin{align*} \text{TSA} = \amp 2 \pi r h + 2 \pi r^2\\ =\amp {\color{blue} 2 \pi r(h+r)} \end{align*}
    \(\textbf{Note:}\) A cylinder is always considered closed, unless it is specified that it is open.

    Example 3.2.13.

    Calculate the surface area of the cylinder shown.
    Solution.
    Given that, \(h=30\,cm \quad \text{and} \quad d=24\,cm\)
    We first get the radius of the cylinder which is given by,
    \begin{align*} r=\amp\frac{d}{2} \\ =\amp \frac{24}{2} \\ =\amp 12\,cm \end{align*}
    Therefore,
    \begin{align*} \text{Surface area}=\amp 2\pi r^2+2\pi r h\\ =\amp 2\pi r(r+h)\\ =\amp 2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times 12\,cm\,(12\,cm+30\,cm ) \\ =\amp 2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times 12\,cm\,\times 42\,cm \\ =\amp 2 \times 22 \times 12 \times 6\\ =\amp 3\,168 \,cm^2 \end{align*}
    The surface area of the above cylinder is \({\color{blue} 3\,168 \,cm^2}\)

    \({\color{black} \textbf{Key Takeaway:}}\)
    A \(\textbf{triangular prism}\) is a three-dimensional shape with two triangular faces and three rectangular faces.
    It belongs to the family of prisms and has a total of \(5\) faces, \(9\) edges, and \(6 \) vertices.
    The figure below shows the triangular prism showing the rectangular faces and the rectangular faces.
    The total surface area of a triangular prism is the sum of the areas of all the faces of the prism.
    The rectangular faces are said to be the \(\textbf{lateral faces}\text{,}\) while the triangular faces are called \(\textbf{bases}\text{.}\) If the bases of a triangular prism are placed horizontally, they are referred to as the top and the bottom (faces) of the prism, respectively.
    \(\textbf{Formula for Surface Area of Triangular Prism}\)
    The triangular prism formula for surface area is formed by adding up the area of all the rectangular and triangular faces of a prism.
    \(\textbf{Note:}\) The area of a triangle is, \(A=\frac{1}{2}bh\) and for the rectangle is, \(A=l \times w\text{.}\)
    Where, \(b=\textit{base of the triangle} \,,\,h=\textit{height of the triangle} \, ,\,l=\textit{length of the rectangle} \,\textbf{and}\, w=\textit{width of the rectangle}\)
    Example 3.2.19.
    Find the surface area of the rectangular prism shown.
    Solution.
    \(\textbf{Surface area}= \textbf{Area of the two triangles} + \textbf{Area of three triangels}\)
    The sides of the triangular base are:
    • \(\displaystyle b_1=3\,in\)
    • \(\displaystyle b_2=4\,in\)
    • \(\displaystyle b_3=5\,in\)
    The triangular base is a right triangle, and so the height is one of our sides. We will use \(h=3\,in.\)
    The length of the prism is \(l=6\,in\)
    Calculate the area of a triangular base using the formula for the area of a triangle: \(A=\frac{1}{2}bh\text{,}\) where \(b\) is base of the triangle. Multiply this result by 2, since there are two triangular bases with this area.
    \begin{align*} A=\amp \frac{1}{2}bh\\ =\amp \frac{1}{2}\times 4 \times 3\\ =\amp 6\,in^2 \end{align*}
    Multiplying this by 2, since we have two triangular bases, we have that the area of the bases is:
    \begin{align*} A=\amp 2 \times 6\,in.^2\\ = \amp {\color{green} 12\,in^2} \end{align*}
    Find the area of the rectangular sides. then add the whole area
    \begin{align*} A=\amp (4 \,in \times 6\,in)+(6\,in\times 5\,in)+(3\,in \times 6\,in) \\ =\amp 24+30+18 \\ = \amp 72\,in^2 \end{align*}
    Therefore.
    \begin{align*} \text{Surface area }=\amp {\color{green} 24\,in^2}+72\,in^2 \\ = \amp 84 \, in^2 \end{align*}
