    Section 1.3 Decimals

    Objectives

    By the end of this substrand, you should be able to:
    1. Convert fractions into decimals.
    2. Convert recurring decimals into fractions.
    3. Round off decimals to the required number of decimal places.
    4. Express numbers using the correct number of significant figures.
    5. Write numbers in standard form.

    Subsection 1.3.1 Conversion of fractions to decimals

    Activity 1.3.1.

    Work in groups
    1. Complete the following table below by following the steps below:
      1. Convert the fractions into equivalent fractions with a denominator that is a power of \(10\) \((\text{ e.g. }, 10, 100, 1000).\)
      2. Simplify the new fraction to decimal form.
      Fraction Equivalence fraction Decimal
      \(\frac{3}{20}\) \(\frac{3 \times 5}{20 \times 5} = \frac{15}{100}\) \(0.15\)
      \(\frac{7}{4}\)
      \(\frac{51}{250}\)
      \(\frac{9}{25}\)
      \(\frac{3}{50}\)
    2. Share your completed table with the rest of the class. Discuss how you converted the fractions and determined their decimal equivalents.
    \(\textbf{Key Takeaway}\)
    To convert a fraction into a decimal, we divide the numerator by the denominator. For instance, to find the decimal equivalent of a fraction such as \(\frac{1}{2}\text{,}\) divide \(1\) by \(2\text{,}\) which gives \(1 \div 2 = 0.5\text{.}\)
    Alternatively, we can convert a fraction to a decimal by writing its equivalence fraction with a denominator which is divisible by \(10.\)
    • Identify a number that, when multiplied by the denominator, results in \(10, 100, 1000,\) or any number that is \(1\) followed by zeros.
    • Multiply both the numerator and the denominator by that number to create an equivalent fraction with a denominator that is a power of \(10\text{.}\)
    • Write down the numerator of the new fraction. Move the decimal point to the left by as many places as there are zeros in the denominator.

    Example 1.3.1.

    Convert the following fractions to decimals.
    1. \(\displaystyle \frac{7}{8}\)
    2. \(\displaystyle \frac{9}{5}\)
    Solution.
    1. \(\frac{7}{8}\)
      Multiply both the numerator and the denominator by \(125\) to make the denominator a power of \(10\text{.}\)
      \begin{equation*} \frac{7}{8} = \frac{7 \times 125}{8 \times 125} = \frac{875}{1000} \end{equation*}
      Write down \(875\) with the decimal point \(3\) spaces from the right (because \(1000\) has \(3\) zeros)
      \begin{align*} \amp = \frac{875}{1000} \\ \amp \\ \amp = 0.875 \end{align*}
    2. \(\frac{9}{5}\)
      Multiply both the numerator and the denominator by \(2\) to make the denominator a power of \(10\text{.}\)
      \begin{equation*} \frac{9}{5} = \frac{9 \times 2}{5 \times 2} = \frac{18}{10} \end{equation*}
      Write down \(18\) with the decimal point \(1\) space from the right (because \(10\) has one zero)
      \begin{align*} \amp= \frac{18}{10} \\ \amp \\ \amp= 1.8 \end{align*}

    Subsection 1.3.2 Recurring decimals

    Activity 1.3.2.

    Work in groups
    1. Using the long division method, convert the fraction \(\frac{6}{7}\) to a decimal number upto \(12\) decimal places.
    2. What do you notice about the division process, does the division end or it continues infinitely?
    3. Identify the sequence of digits that repeat themselves after the decimal point.
    4. What do we call a decimal number where some digits repeat infinitely without ending?
    5. Provide another example of a recurring decimal.
    6. Share your findings with the rest of learners in the class.
    \(\textbf{Key Takeaway}\)
    A recurring number, also known as a repeating decimal, is a decimal number in which a specific digit or group of digits after the decimal point repeats without ending.
    In recurring decimals, dot notation is used to indicate repeating digits. A dot placed above a specific digit or sequence of digits shows which part repeats. For instance, \(0.5\dot{7}\) represents \(0.577777...\text{,}\) and \(0.\dot{2}\dot{7}\) represents \(0.272727...\)

    Example 1.3.2.

    Identify whether the following decimals is recurring or non-recurring.
    1. \(\displaystyle 0.3333...\)
    2. \(\displaystyle 0.123123123...\)
    3. \(\displaystyle 1.25\)
    4. \(\displaystyle 0.142857142857...\)
    Solution.
    1. \(0.3333...\) is recurring because the digit \(3\) repeats continuously without ending.
    2. \(0.123123123...\) is recurring because the digits \(123\) repeats continuously without ending.
    3. \(1.25\) is non recurring because the digits \(2\) and \(5\) do not recur.
    4. \(0.142857142857...\) is recurring because the digits \(142857\) repeats continuously without ending.

    Subsection 1.3.3 Converting Recurring Decimals to Fractions

    Activity 1.3.3.

    Work in groups
    1. Convert \(0.\dot{1}\) into a fraction.
    2. Describe the method or strategy you used to convert \(0.\dot{1}\) into a fraction.
    3. Discuss any challenges you faced and how you overcame them.
    4. Share and discuss with other learners about your findings.
    \(\textbf{ Key Takeaway }\)
    In the previous lesson, Subsection 1.3.2, we explored recurring decimals. Now, we will learn how to convert recurring decimals into fractions.
    To do this, we start by defining the decimal as an unknown variable, say \(x\text{,}\) and label this as equation \(1\text{.}\) Next, we multiply both sides of equation \(1\) by \(10^n\text{,}\) where \(n\) represents the number of repeating digits, and label this as equation \(2\text{.}\) Finally, we subtract equation \(1\) from equation \(2\) to eliminate the recurring part and solve for \(x\text{.}\)

    Example 1.3.3.

    Express each of the following as a fraction.
    1. \(\displaystyle 0.\dot{2}\)
    2. \(\displaystyle 0.\dot{6}\dot{3}\)
    Solution.
    1. \(0.\dot{2}\)
      Step \(1:\) To convert the \(0.\dot{2}\) into a fraction, we let \(0.\dot{2}\) in the expanded form be equal to unkown variable, say, \(x\text{.}\)
      Now, \(x = 0.2222...\)
      Step \(2\text{:}\) Identify the repeating digits.
      In this case we have only one repeating digit which is \(2.\) We therefore multiply both sides of the equation in step \(1\) by \(10^1 = 10.\)
      \begin{equation*} 10 x = 2.222... \end{equation*}
      Step \(3:\) Subtract the result of step \(1\) from the result of step \(2\) to eliminate the recurring part.
      \(10x\) \(=\) \(2.2222...\)
      \(- x\) \(=\) \(0.2222...\)
      \(9x\) \(=\) \(2\)
      Solve for \(x\text{,}\) by dividing both side of the equation by \(9.\)
      \begin{align*} \frac{\cancel{9}x}{\cancel{9}} \amp = \frac{2}{9} \\ \amp \\ x \amp = \frac{2}{9} \end{align*}
      Therefore, \(0.\dot{2} = \frac{2}{9}\)
    2. \(0.\dot{6}\dot{3}\)
      We equate the recurring decimal to a variable, say \(x\text{,}\) to create equation \((1)\)
      \begin{equation*} x = 0.6363... \qquad \qquad \qquad \text{equation}(1) \end{equation*}
      Since \(0.6363...\) has two repeating digits, we multiply both sides of equation\((1)\) by \(100\) so that the recurring digit of the decimals align (this creates equation \((2)\))
      \begin{equation*} 100x = 63.63... \qquad \qquad \qquad \text{equation}(2) \end{equation*}
      Subtract equation\((1)\) from equation\((2)\)
      \(100x\) \(=\) \(63.63...\)
      \(- x\) \(=\) \(0.63...\)
      \(99x\) \(=\) \(63\)
      Solve for \(x\text{,}\) by dividing both side of the equation by \(99.\)
      \begin{align*} 99x \amp= 63 \\ \amp \\ \frac{99x}{99} \amp= \frac{63}{99} \\ \amp \\ \amp= \frac{63}{99} \end{align*}
      Simplify the fraction
      \(\frac{\cancel{63}^7}{\cancel{99}_{11}} = \frac{7}{11}\)
      Therefore, \(0.\dot{6}\dot{3} = \frac{7}{11}\)

    Subsection 1.3.4 Rounding off decimal numbers

    Activity 1.3.4.

    Work in groups
    1. Using digits \(1,2,3,4,5,6,7,8,9,\) create a \(6\) digit number and write it down on a piece of paper.
    2. Divide the number created in step (b) by \(10000\text{.}\)
    3. Round off the resulting number to correct \(3\) decimal place.
    4. Share and discuss your calculations with the rest of learners in the class.
    \(\textbf{ Key Takeaway }\)
    To round a decimal number to a specified number of decimal places, we examine the digit immediately after the desired decimal place. If this digit is less than \(5\text{,}\) simply remove all subsequent digits. If the digit is \(5\) or greater, we add \(1\) to the digit in the desired decimal and eliminate the following digits.

    Example 1.3.4.

    Round off each of the following to one decimal place:
    1. \(\displaystyle 45.672\)
    2. \(\displaystyle 1.712\)
    Solution.
    1. \(45.672\)
      We first place each digit in its correct place value position.
      Number Tens Ones Decimal point Tenths Hundredths Thousandths
      \(45.672\) \(4\) \(5\) \(.\) \(6\) \(7\) \(2\)
      To round \(45.672\) to one decimal place, we examine the digit in the hundredths place.
      The hundredths digit is \(7\text{,}\) which is greater than \(5\text{.}\) Therefore, we add \(1\) to the tenths digit.
      Thus, \(45.672\) rounded to one decimal place is \(45.7\text{.}\)
    2. \(1.712\)
      We first place each digit in its correct place value position.
      Number Ones Decimal point Tenths Hundredths Thousandths
      \(1.712\) \(1\) \(.\) \(7\) \(1\) \(2\)
      To round \(1.712\) to one decimal place, we examine the digit in the hundredths place.
      The hundredths digit is \(1\text{,}\) which is less than \(5\text{.}\) Therefore, we don’t add \(1\) to the tenths digit.
      Thus, \(1.712\) rounded to one decimal place is \(1.7\text{.}\)

    Example 1.3.5.

    Round off the following mass in kilograms correct to three decimal places:
    1. \(4.7823\) kg
    Solution.
    1. \(4.7823\) kg
      We first place each digit in its correct place value position.
      Number Ones Decimal point Tenths Hundredths Thousandths Hundred thousandths
      \(4.7823\) \(4\) \(.\) \(7\) \(8\) \(2\) \(3\)
      To round \(4.7823\) to three decimal place, we examine the digit in the hundred thousandths place.
      The hundred thousandths digit is \(3\text{,}\) which is less than \(5\text{.}\) Therefore, we don’t add \(1\) to the thousandths digit.
      Thus, \(4.7823\) rounded to three decimal place is \(4.782\text{.}\)

    Subsection 1.3.5 Expressing Numbers to the Required Significant Figures

    Activity 1.3.5.

    Work in groups
    1. Consider the following numbers: \(0.00123,\,78.0089,\,12.3100,\)
    2. Find and identify the significant digits in each of the numbers above.
    3. Express each number in step (a) to \(4\) significant figures.
    4. Share and discuss your findings with the rest of learners in the class.
    \(\textbf{Key Takeaway}\)
    Significant figures (or significant digits) are the digits in a number that contribute to its precision. They include all non-zero digits, any zeros between non-zero digits, and trailing zeros in decimal numbers.
    \(\textbf{Learning Point}\)
    • All nonzero digits are significant.
    • All zeros between nonzero digits are significant.
    • Leading zeros (to the left of the first nonzero digit) are not significant.
    • Trailing zeros to the right of the decimal place are significant.
    • Zero appearing after non-zero digit. On the whole numbers side may be significant or may not be significant figures. It can be a place holder or a significant.

    Example 1.3.6.

    How many significant figures are there in the following masses?
    1. \(45.023\) kg
    2. \(120\) kg
    3. \(145.60\) kg
    4. \(0.0023\) kg
    Solution.
    1. \(45.023\) kg
      All nonzero digits are significant and zeros between them count as significant.
      Thus \(45.023\) kg contains five significant digits.
    2. \(120\) kg
      All the zeros that are on the right of the last non-zero digit are significant if they come from a measurement.
      Thus \(120\) kg contains three significant digits.
    3. \(145.60\) kg
      All the zeros that are on the right of the last non-zero digit, after the decimal point, are significant.
      Thus \(145.60\) kg contains five significant digits.
    4. \(0.0023\) kg
      Leading zeros are never significant
      Thus \(0.0023\) kg contains two significant digits.

    Example 1.3.7.

    Express each of the following numbers to the given number of significant figures in brackets:
    1. \(\displaystyle 12891 \quad (3)\)
    2. \(\displaystyle 0.02789 \quad (2)\)
    Solution.
    1. \(12891 \quad (3)\)
      The number \(12891\) has five significant digits.
      To round it off to three significant figures, we consider the fourth significant digit.
      The fourth significant digit is \(9\text{,}\) which is greater than \(5\text{.}\) Therefore, we add \(1\) to the third significant digit and replace the fourth and fifth significant digits with zeros.
      Thus, \(12891\) to three significant figures is \(12900\text{.}\)
      In this case, zeros serve as placeholders and not significant figures.
    2. \(0.02789 \quad (2)\)
      The number \(0.02789\) has four significant digits because the leading zeros are not significant digits.
      To round it off to two significant digits, we consider the third significant digit.
      The third significant digit is \(8\text{,}\) which is greater than \(5\text{.}\) Therefore, we add \(1\) to the second significant digit and we remove the remaining digits.
      Thus, \(0.02789\) correct to \(2\) significant digit is \(0.028\)

    Subsection 1.3.6 Expressing Numbers in Standard Form

    Activity 1.3.6.

    Work in groups
    1. Using digits \(1,2,3,4,5,6,7,8,9,\) create a \(4\) digit number and write it down on a piece of paper.
    2. Starting from the digit in the ones place, move \(3\) steps to the left and insert a decimal point to form a decimal number.
    3. Multiply the decimal number obtained in the previous step by \(10\) raised to the power of the number of steps moved.
    4. Share your final number in standard form with the rest of learners in your class.
    \(\textbf{ Key Takeaway }\)
    Standard form is a way of representing numbers in a clear and consistent way. A number expressed in the form of \(p \times 10^n\text{,}\) where \(n\) is an integer and \(p\) is greater than zero but less than \(10 \) is said to be in standard form.

    Example 1.3.8.

    Express each of the following numbers in standard from:
    1. \(\displaystyle 5679\)
    2. \(\displaystyle 0.000712\)
    3. \(\displaystyle 1236.9721\)
    Solution.
    1. \(5679\)
      To express \(5679\) in standard form, we start by writing the first digit of the number.
      The first digit of \(5679\) is \(5\text{.}\) We then place a decimal point after it and write the remaining digits, giving \(5.679\text{.}\)
      Since we moved the decimal point \(3\) places to the left, we multiply by \(10^{3}\text{.}\)
      Therefore, the standard form of \(5679\) is \(5.679 \times 10^{3}\text{.}\)
    2. \(0.000712\)
      To express \(0.000712\) in standard from, we start by identifying the first non-zero digit
      The first non-zero digit in \(0.000712\) is \(7\text{.}\)
      Next, we place the decimal point immediately after \(7\text{,}\) giving \(7.12\text{.}\)
      Since we shifted the decimal point \(4\) places to the right, we express this as an exponent of \(-4\) with base \(10\text{.}\)
      Therefore, the standard form of \(0.000712\) is \(7.12 \times 10^{-4}\text{.}\)
    3. \(1236.9721\)
      To express \(1236.9721\) in standard form, we begin by identifying the first non-zero digit.
      The first non-zero digit in \(1236.9721\) is \(1\text{.}\)
      Next, we place the decimal point immediately after \(1\text{,}\) giving \(1.2369721\text{.}\)
      Since we shifted the decimal point \(3\) places to the left, we express this as an exponent of \(3\) with base \(10\text{.}\)
      Therefore, the standard form of \(1236.9721\) is \(1.2369721 \times 10^{3}\text{.}\)

    Subsection 1.3.7 Combined Operations

    Activity 1.3.7.

    Work in groups
    Materials Needed: A \(30\) cm ruler.
    1. Measure the length of an A\(4\) exercise book in centimeters.
    2. Measure the width of the A\(4\) exercise book in centimeters.
    3. Measure the length of a pen in centimeters.
    4. Measure the length of the teacher’s board in centimeters.
    5. Convert all the measurements from centimeters to meters by dividing each by \(100\text{.}\)
    6. Divide the board’s length in meters by the length of the A\(4\) exercise book. Multiply the result by the width of the A\(4\) exercise book. Finally, add the length of the pen to the result.
    7. Share and discuss your findings with the rest of learners in class.
    \(\textbf{Key Takeaway }\)
    Combined operations on decimals involve performing multiple arithmetic operations such as addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division within a single expression. We follow the order of brackets, division, multiplication, addition, and finally Subtraction (BODMAS) when working out multiple operations.

    Example 1.3.9.

    Work out each of the following:
    1. \(\displaystyle 4.85 - 56.45 + 89.75\)
    2. \(\displaystyle 2.5 \times 5.1 \div 1.7 - 3.9 + 7.5\)
    Solution.
    1. \(4.85 - 56.45 + 89.75\)
      We first perform addition, then subtraction.
      We rearrange the operation as \(4.85 + 89.75 - 56.45 \)
      To add decimal numbers, align them vertically.
      \(4.85\)
      \(+\,89.75\)
      \(94.60\)
      Then, we perform the subtraction. \(94.60 - 56.45\)
      \(94.60\)
      \(-\,56.45\)
      \(38.15\)
      Thus, \(4.85 - 56.45 + 89.75 = 38.15\)
    2. Given the expression:
      \begin{equation*} 2.5 \times 5.1 \div 1.7 - 3.9 + 6.5 \end{equation*}
      Perform the division first.
      \begin{equation*} 5.1 \div 1.7 \end{equation*}
      Express the decimals as fractions and then we multiply the dividend by the reciprocal of the divisor:
      \(\frac{51}{10} \div \frac{17}{10}= \frac{51}{10} \times \frac{10}{17} = \frac{51 \times 10}{10 \times 17} = \frac{510}{170} = 3\)
      Now, the expression simplifies to:
      \begin{equation*} 2.5 \times 3 - 3.9 + 6.5 \end{equation*}
      Perform the multiplication:
      \begin{equation*} 2.5 \times 3 = \frac{25}{10} \times 3 = \frac{25 \times 3}{10} = \frac{75}{10} = 7.5 \end{equation*}
      Now, the expression reduces to:
      \begin{equation*} 7.5 - 3.9 + 6.5 \end{equation*}
      Perform the addition:
      \begin{equation*} 7.5 + 6.5 = 14 \end{equation*}
      subtract \(3.9\) from \(14.\)
      \(14 - 3.9 = 10.1 \)
