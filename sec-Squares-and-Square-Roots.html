    Section 1.4 Squares and Square Roots

    Akinyi, a Grade \(8\) student, uses squares and square roots to help her family plan their vegetable garden. Her parents want to create a square garden with an area of \(36\) square meters. To determine the length of each side, Akinyi calculates the square root of \(36\text{,}\) which is \(6\) meters. This allows her family to know they need \(4 \times 6 = 24\) meters of fencing to enclose the garden.
    Squares and square roots are essential tools in solving real-world problems. Learners use them to calculate unknown lengths in triangles using the Pythagorean theorem, find areas, and design structures that require precise measurements.

    Subsection 1.4.1 Squares of whole numbers using tables

    In this section, we will work out squares of whole numbers using square tables.
    Square tables consist of \(3\) sections:
    1. Section with column headed \(x\) containing numbers from \(1.0-9.9\text{.}\) Therefore, if a number is not within this range, first write the number in standard form before identifying its square from the table.
    2. Section with columns headed \(0-9\)
    3. Section labelled ADD with columns headed \(0-9\)
    Squares of numbers using square tables are approximations, therefore, answers are slightly different from those by long multiplication.

    \(\textbf{ How to read and calculate squares of numbers using a table.}\)
    Calculate the square of :
    1. \(\displaystyle 2\)
    2. \(\displaystyle 16\)
    3. \(\displaystyle 1345\)
    \(\textbf{Solution}\)
      1. Move down the column headed \(x\) to locate \(2.0\text{.}\)
      2. Move to the right along the row that has \(2.0\) to the column headed \(0\) i.e to check for the square of \(2\) you look at \(2.00\)
      3. Read the number at the intersection of the row and the column.
        Therefore, the square of \(2\) from the square table is \(4.000\)
      1. Write the number \(16\) in standard form to get \(1.6 \times 10\text{.}\) Hence \(16^2 = (1.6 \times 10)^2 = 1.6^2 \times 10^2\)
      2. To find \(1.6^2\) ,move down the column headed \(x\) to locate \(1.6\)
      3. Move to the right along the row that has \(1.6\) to the column headed \(0\)
      4. Read the number at the intersection of the row and the column.
      5. The square of \(16 = 2.560 \times 10^2 = 2.560 \times 100\)
        \(\quad\quad = 256\)
      1. Write the number \(1345\) in standard form to get \(1.345 \times 10^3. \) Therefore, \(1.345^2 \times (10^3)^2\text{.}\)
      2. Move down the column headed \(x\) to locate \(1.3\text{.}\)
      3. Move to the right along the row that has \(1.3\) to the column headed \(4\)
      4. Read the number at the intersection of the row and the column which is \(1.796\)
      5. Move farther to the right along the row that has \(1.3\) to the ADD column headed \(5\text{.}\)
      6. Read the number at the intersection of the row and the column \(=13\text{.}\)
      7. Add the numbers you got in step (iv) and (vi) above from the right i.e \(1.796 + 0.013 = 1.809\)
        Therefore, \(1.345^2 \times (10^3)^2 = 1.809\times 1000 000\)
        Square of \(1345\) from the square table \(= 1 809 000\)

    Subsection 1.4.2 Squares of decimal numbers using tables

    To find the square of a decimal number:
    1. Write the decimal number in standard form e.g given \(0.154\) writing it in standard form =\(1.54 \times 10^{-1}\)
    2. Move down the column headed \(x\) to locate \(1.5\text{.}\)
    3. Move to the right along the row that has \(1.5\) to the column headed \(4\text{.}\)
    4. Read the number at the intersection of the row and the column.
      The number is \(2.372\)
    5. Calculate the square of \(10^{-1} \text{.}\)
      Since \(10^-1 = \frac{1}{10}\text{,}\) then \((10^{-1})^2 = \frac{1}{10} \times \frac{1}{10}\)
    6. Multiply the square of \(1.54\) by the square of \(10^{-1}\)
    A number raised to a negative power implies that the number is a fraction or a decimal.
    Example: \(10^{-1} = \frac{1}{10}\text{,}\) also written as \(0.1\)
    \(\quad\quad\quad 10^{-2}= \frac{1}{100}\text{,}\) also written as \(0.01\)
