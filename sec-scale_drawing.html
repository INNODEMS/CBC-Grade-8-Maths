    Section 4.3 Scale Drawing

    \(\textbf{A scale drawing}\) is a proportional representation of an object or place, where all dimensions are reduced or enlarged using a specific ratio while maintaining their correct proportions.

    Subsection 4.3.1 Representing length to a given scale

    Activity 4.3.1.

    \(\textbf{Work in groups}\)
    The teacher provided the learners with a textbook, a ruler, and an exercise book for the activity.
    1. Individually, draw the textbook accurately in your exercise book.
    2. How different is the length of your drawing from the actual length of the textbook?
    3. Measure the actual length of the textbook and the length of your drawing in centimetres.
    4. Share your work with the other groups and compare results.

    Activity 4.3.2.

    \(\textbf{Work in groups}\)
    What you need:
    • Ruler or measuring tape
    • Exercise book or plain paper
    • Pencil
    1. Students individually observe the classroom window and door and estimate its height and width in centimeters.
    2. Using the ruler or measuring tape, accurately measure the actual height and width of the window and door in centimeters.
    3. Recordyour measurement in the table shown below.
    4. Draw a scaled-down version of the height and width of window and door in the exercise books using a scale of 1cm represents 15cm.
    5. Record your results in the table below
    6. Share and discuss your results with other groups.
    \(\textbf{Key Takeaway}\)
    • \(\textbf{Representing length to a given scale}\) involves creating a proportional representation of real-world measurements, typically in drawings, maps, or models.
    • \(\textbf{A scale}\) is a ratio that compares the dimensions of a representation (like a drawing) to the corresponding dimensions of the actual object. It indicates how much smaller or larger the representation is compared to reality.

    Example 4.3.3.

    The length of a school field is measured as \(12000 \textbf{ cm}\text{.}\) Given that \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) represents \(600 \textbf{ cm}\text{,}\) represent the length of the school field on paper.
    Solution.
    Actual length of a school field = \(12000 \textbf{ cm}\text{.}\)
    Scale = \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) represents \(600 \textbf{ cm}\text{.}\)
    To find the length on paper, we use the formula:
    \begin{align*} \textbf{Drawing Length} = \amp \frac{\textbf{Actual Length}}{\textbf{Scale Factor}}\\ = \amp \frac{12,000}{600}\\ = \amp 20 \textbf{ cm} \end{align*}
    Therefore, The length of the school field on paper will be \(20 \textbf{ cm}\text{.}\)

    Example 4.3.4.

    The height of a mountain is \(3,500 \textbf{ meters}\text{.}\) If we use a scale where \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) represents \(500 \textbf{ meters}\text{,}\) represent the height of the mountain in your workbook.
    Solution.
    Actual height of the mountain = \(3500 \textbf{ meters}\)
    Scale = \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) represents \(500 \textbf{ meters}\)
    To find the height of the mountain on paper, we use the formula:
    \begin{align*} \textbf{Drawing Height} = \amp \frac{\textbf{Actual height }}{\textbf{Scale factor}}\\ = \amp \frac{3500}{500}\\ = \amp 7 \textbf{ cm} \end{align*}
    Therefore, the height of the mountain would be drawn as \(7 \textbf{ cm}\) tall on paper.

    Example 4.3.5.

    A map has a scale of \(1:75,000\text{.}\) A road is \(6 \textbf{ kilometers}\) long in reality. How long should it be drawn on the map in centimeters?
    Solution.
    Scale = \(1:75,000\) (This means \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) on the map represents \(75,000 \textbf{ cm}\) in reality.)
    Actual road length = \(6 \textbf{ km}\)
    Convert kilometers to centimeters:
    \begin{align*} \amp \frac{6 \cancel{ km}}{1 \cancel{ km}} \times 100,000 \textbf{ cm}\\ = \amp 600,000\textbf{ cm} \end{align*}
    Now, we use the formula:
    \begin{align*} \textbf{Drawing Length} = \amp \frac{\textbf{Actual Length}}{\textbf{Scale Factor}}\\ = \amp \frac{600,000}{75,000}\\ = \amp 8 \textbf{ cm} \end{align*}
    The road should be drawn as \(8 \textbf{ cm}\) long on the map.

    Activity 4.3.3.

    \(\textbf{Work in groups}\)
    1. Use a measuring tape or ruler to find the actual height and width of an object in your classroom (e.g., door, window, or notice board).
    2. Convert the actual measurements into scale measurements using the following scales:
      • \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) represents \(5 \textbf{ cm}\text{.}\)
      • \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) represents \(15 \textbf{ cm}\text{.}\)
      • \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) represents \(40 \textbf{ cm}\text{.}\)
      • \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) represents \(8 \textbf{ m}\text{.}\)
    3. Fill in the table below with your results.
    4. Discuss how different scales affect the drawing size and explain which scale you think is best for representing large objects on paper.
    \(\textbf{Key Takeaway}\)
    • \(\textbf{Converting actual length to scale length}\) involves reducing or enlarging real-world measurements based on a given scale ratio to fit on a drawing or map.
    • \(\textbf{Actual length}\) is the real-world length of the same object.
    • \(\textbf{Scale length}\) refers to the length of an object as represented in the drawing or model.
    • To covert actual length to scale length, the actual measurement is divided by the specified scale factor.
      \begin{align*} \textbf{Scale Length} = \amp \frac{\textbf{Actual length}}{\textbf{Scale Factor}} \end{align*}

    Example 4.3.7.

    The length of a building is \(15 \textbf{ m}\text{.}\) Nanjala drew the building using a scale where \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) represents \(3 \textbf{ m} \text{.}\) Determine the length of the drawing.
    Solution.
    The actual length of the building = \(15 \textbf{ m}\)
    Scale = \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) represents \(3 \textbf{ m} \)
    To find the length of the drawing, we use:
    \begin{align*} \textbf{Drawing Length} = \amp \frac{\textbf{Actual Length}}{\textbf{Scale Factor}} \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} \amp \frac{15 \cancel{ m}}{3 \cancel{ m}} \times 1 \textbf{ cm}\\ = \amp 5 \textbf{ cm} \end{align*}
    Therefore, The length of the drawing is \(5 \textbf{ cm}\)

    Example 4.3.8.

    Calculate the scale length that corresponds to an actual bridge length of 120 meters for the given scales;
    1. \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) represents \(20 \textbf{ m} \)
    2. \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) represents \(1 \textbf{ km} \)
    3. \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) represents \(80 \textbf{ m} \)
    4. \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) represents \(100 \textbf{ m} \)
    Solution.
    Actual length of the bridge = \(120 \textbf{ m}\)
    1. \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) represents \(20 \textbf{ m} \)
      \begin{align*} \amp \frac{120 \cancel{ m}}{20 \cancel{ m}} \times 1 \textbf{cm}\\ = \amp 6 \textbf{ cm} \end{align*}
      The scale length is, \(6 \textbf{ cm}\)
    2. \(1 \textbf{ccm}\) represents \(1 \textbf{ km} \)
      First, we need to convert \(\textbf{ km}\) to \(\textbf{ m}\)
      Since, \(1 \textbf{ km}\) = \(1000 \textbf{ m} \text{,}\) we convert:
      \begin{align*} \amp \frac{1 \cancel{ km}}{1 \cancel{ km}} \times 1000 \textbf{ m}\\ = \amp 1000 \textbf{ m} \end{align*}
      Therefore \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) represents \(1000 \textbf{ m} \)
      \begin{align*} \amp \frac{120 \textbf{ m}}{1000 \textbf{ m}} = 0.12\textbf{ cm} \end{align*}
      The scale length is , \(0.12 \textbf{ cm}\)
    3. \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) represents \(80 \textbf{ m} \)
      \begin{align*} \amp \frac{120 \cancel{ m}}{80 \cancel{ m}} \times 1 \textbf{ cm}\\ = \amp 1.5 \textbf{ cm} \end{align*}
      The scale length is, \(1.5 \textbf{ cm}\)
    4. \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) represents \(100 \textbf{ m} \)
      \begin{align*} \amp \frac{120 \cancel{ m}}{100 \cancel{ m}} \times 1 \textbf{ cm}\\ = \amp 1.2 \textbf{ cm} \end{align*}
      The scale length is, \(1.2 \textbf{ cm}\)

    Example 4.3.9.

    A map is drawn to a scale of \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) represents \(5 \textbf{ km} \text{.}\) Two towns A and B are \(200 \textbf{ km}\) apart. What is the distance between the two towns on the map.
    Solution.
    Actual distance between towns A and B: \(200 \textbf{ km}\)
    Scale: \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) represents \(5 \textbf{ km} \)
    \(\textbf{Map distance}\) = \(\textbf{Actual ditance} \div \textbf{Scale factor}\)
    \begin{align*} \amp \frac{200 \cancel{km}}{5 \cancel{ km}} \times 1 \textbf{ cm}\\ = \amp 40 \textbf{ cm} \end{align*}
    Therefore, Therefore, the distance between towns A and B on the map is \(40 \textbf{ cm}\)

    • To convert scale length to actual length, multiply the scale length by the specified scale factor.
      \begin{align*} \textbf{Actual length} = \amp \textbf{Scale length} \times \textbf{Scale factor} \end{align*}

    Example 4.3.11.

    A road is represented by a line measuring \(4 \textbf{ cm}\) on a scale drawing. The scale used is \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) represents \(5 \textbf{ m}\text{.}\) Find the actual length of the road.

    the scale of the drawing = \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) represents \(5 \textbf{ m}\)

    \begin{align*} \textbf{Actual length} = \amp \textbf{Scale length} \times \textbf{Scale factor}\\ = \amp 4 \textbf{ m}\times 5 \textbf{ m}\\ = \amp 20 \textbf{ m} \end{align*}
    Therefore, the actual length of the road is \(20 \textbf{ m}\text{.}\)

    Example 4.3.12.

    The actual diameter of the lake is \(12 \textbf{ km}\text{.}\)

    Subsection 4.3.4 Interpreting linear scales in statement form

    Activity 4.3.5.

    \(\textbf{Work in groups}\)
    1. Given a measuring tape or ruler, grid paper and a pencil to complete the activity.
    2. Measure the length, width and height of your desk in cm.
    3. Given the distance between each desk in a classroom is \(50 \textbf{ cm}\text{.}\)
    4. Find the scale used in the statement form.
    5. Discuss with the other groups.
    \(\textbf{Key Takeaway}\)
    • \(\textbf{Interpreting a linear scale in statement form}\) means understanding the relationship between distances on a map (or drawing) and the corresponding distances on the real world using words.
    • \(\textbf{A linear scale}\text{,}\) also known as a bar scale or graphic scale, is a visual representation of the relationship between map distances and real-world distances.
    • The scale is often expressed in a statement, such as "every 1 cm on the drawing corresponds to an actual distance of 50 meters in reality.

    Example 4.3.14.

    The distance between two mountain peaks on a map is \(15 \textbf{ cm}\text{.}\)
    1. If the actual distance between the two peaks is \(45 \textbf{ km}\text{,}\) interpret the scale and write it in a statement form.
    2. If the actual distance between two other mountain peaks along the same range is \(60 \textbf{ km}\text{,}\) determine the scale of the distance between them on the map.
    Solution.
    Distance between two mountain peaks on the map = \(15 \textbf{ cm}\text{.}\)
    Actual distance between the two peaks = \(45 \textbf{ km}\text{.}\)
    1. Interpreting the Scale
      To find the scale, we divide the actual distance by the map distance:
      Convert \(45 \textbf{ km}\) to \(\textbf{cm}\)
      Since \(1 \textbf{ km}\) = \(100,000 \textbf{ cm}\)
      \begin{align*} \amp \frac{45 \cancel{ km}}{1 \cancel{ km}} \times 100,000 \textbf{ cm}\\ = \amp 4,500,000 \textbf{ cm} \end{align*}
      \begin{align*} \textbf{Scale} = \amp \frac{ \textbf{Actual Distance}} {\textbf{Map Distance}}\\ \textbf{Scale} = \amp \frac{4,500,000}{15}\\ = \amp 300,000 \textbf{ cm} \end{align*}
      So, the scale is \(1:300,000\text{,}\) meaning \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) on the map represents \(300,000 \textbf{ cm}\) (or \(3 \textbf{ km}\)) in reality.
    2. Finding the Map Distance for 60 km.
      Using the same scale \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) represents \(3 \textbf{ km}\text{,}\) we determine the map distance for an actual distance of \(60 \textbf{ km}\text{:}\)
      \begin{align*} \textbf{Map Distance} = \amp \frac{ \textbf{Actual Distance}} {\textbf{Scale factor}}\\ = \amp \frac{60}{3}\\ = \amp 20 \textbf{ cm} \end{align*}

    Example 4.3.15.

    On a city map, the distance between the library and museum is \(4.5 \textbf{ cm}\text{.}\) If the actual distance is \(900 \textbf{ meters}\text{.}\) Interpret and write the scale in statement form.
    Solution.
    Map distance = \(4.5 \textbf{ cm}\)
    Actual distance = \(900 \textbf{ meters}\)
    Convert meters into cm
    \begin{align*} \amp \frac{900 \cancel{ m}}{1 \cancel{ m}} \times 100 \textbf{ cm}\\ = \amp90,000 \textbf{ cm} \end{align*}
    so, Actual distance = \(90,000 \textbf{ cm}\)
    The formula to find the scale is:
    \begin{align*} \textbf{Scale} = \amp \frac{\textbf{Actual Distance}}{\textbf{ Map Distance}}\\ = \amp \frac{90,000}{4.5}\\ = \amp 20,000 \textbf{ cm} \end{align*}
    So, the scale is \(1:20,000\text{,}\) meaning \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) on the map represents \(20,000 \textbf{ cm}\) (or \(200 \textbf{ m}\)) in reality.

    Subsection 4.3.5 Writing linear scales in statement form

    Activity 4.3.6.

    \(\textbf{Work in groups}\)
    Three stalls, A, B and C in a market is marked on a map. The distance between A and B is \(150 \textbf{ cm}\text{,}\) B and C is \(250 \textbf{ cm}\text{.}\) If the actual distance from A to B is \(1.5 \textbf{ km}\text{.}\)
    1. Express the scale used in statement form.
    2. Find the actual distance between B and C.
    3. Discuss and share with other groups.
    \(\textbf{Key Takeaway}\)
    • Linear scale shows, the relationship between drawing (scale) length and actual length. Linear scale of a diagram is given in statement. To write a linear scale in statement form, you express it as:
      \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) on the map represents \(\textbf{ km}\) (or meters) in real life.
    • A linear scale in statement form indicates what a single unit of measurement on the scale corresponds to in real-world length.

    Example 4.3.16.

    A farmer’s field is \(500 \textbf{ m}\) long. On a map, this length is represented by a \(5 \textbf{ cm}\) line. Work out the scale of the map in statement form.
    Solution.
    Actual length = \(500 \textbf{ m}\)
    Map length = \(5 \textbf{ cm}\)
    Convert \(500 \textbf{ m}\) to \(\textbf{cm}\)
    Since \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) = \(10,000 \textbf{ m}\)
    \begin{align*} \amp \frac{500 \cancel{ m}}{1 \cancel{ m}} \times 10,000 \textbf{ cm}\\ = \amp 50,000 \textbf{ cm} \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} \textbf{Scale} = \amp \frac{ \textbf{Actual Length}} {\textbf{Map Length}}\\ \textbf{Scale} = \amp \frac{50000}{5}\\ = \amp 10,000 \textbf{ cm} \\ = \amp 10,000:1 \end{align*}
    Therefore, in statement form \(1 \textbf{ cm}\) on the map represents \(10,000 \textbf{ cm}\) (or \(100 \textbf{ m}\)) in reality.

    \begin{align*} 1\,m=\amp 100 \, cm\\ 6\,m =\amp \frac{6\,m \times 100 \, cm}{1\,m}\\ = \amp 600\, cm \end{align*}
    Now, applying the scale:
    \begin{align*} 1\,cm=\amp 50\,cm \\ ?=\amp 600\, cm\\ =\amp \frac{600\, cm \times 1\,cm}{50\,cm}\\ =\amp 12 \,cm \end{align*}
    The length of the living room on the blueprint is \({\color{blue}12 \,cm }\text{.}\)
