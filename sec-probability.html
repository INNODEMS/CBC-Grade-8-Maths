    Section 5.2 Probability

    \(\textbf{Probability}\) refers to the likelihood of a specific event happening.

    Subsection 5.2.1 Identifying Events Involving Chance in Real Life Situations

    Activity 5.2.1.

    \(\textbf{Work in groups}\)
    1. Classify the following events as likely, unlikely, must happen and cannot happen.
      • A baby learning to walk by the age of two.
      • It will rain in the rainy season.
      • Getting a number between 1 and 6 when rolling a six-sided die.
      • Tossing a coin and it lands on heads.
      • The school bell ringing at the end of the day.
      • A cat giving birth to puppies.
    3. Discuss and compare with other groups.
    \(\textbf{Key Takeaway}\)
    • \(\textbf{Identifying events involving chance in real life}\) means recognizing situations with unpredictable outcomes influenced by random factors, such as flipping a coin, rolling dice, or forecasting weather.
    • Events can be classified as likely, unlikely, certain (must happen), or impossible (cannot happen).

    Example 5.2.1.

    In a classroom of 40 students, the teacher randomly selects one student to answer a question. Identify an event that is likely,unlikely, must happen and cannot happen.
    1. The chosen student has a pencil.
    2. The chosen student is left-handed.
    3. The teacher will select a student.
    4. The teacher picks a student who is not in the class.
    Solution.
    1. Likely: Most students bring a pencil to class, so this is more likely to happen.
    2. Unlikely: Some students may be left-handed, but the majority are right-handed, making this event less likely.
    3. Must happen: The teacher must choose someone, so this will definitely happen.
    4. Cannot happen: The teacher can only pick from the students present in class, making this impossible.

    Example 5.2.2.

    At a market, a person buys an item from a shop. Classify the following events as likely, unlikely, must happen, or cannot happen:
    1. The person buys fruits or vegetables.
    2. The person buys a cow from a clothing shop.
    3. The person buys something from the market.
    4. The person buys a mobile phone.
    Solution.
    1. Likely: Fruits and vegetables are commonly sold in markets, so it is very likely.
    2. Cannot happen: Clothing shops do not sell cows, so this is impossible.
    3. Must happen: If a person goes to the market to buy something, they will definitely purchase an item.
    4. Unlikely: Some markets may sell phones, but most markets focus on food and household items, making this less likely.

    Example 5.2.3.

    In a school, different events can happen during the day. Classify the following events as likely, unlikely, must happen, or cannot happen:
    1. The school bell rings at the end of the day.
    2. A student forgets their lunch at home.
    3. A teacher flies to class using wings.
    4. Students attend lessons in their classrooms.
    Solution.
    1. Must happen: The bell always rings to signal the end of the school day.
    2. Unlikely: Some students may forget their lunch, but most bring it, so it is possible but not common.
    3. Cannot happen: Humans cannot fly, so this is impossible.
    4. Likely: Students are supposed to attend lessons, so this is very likely to happen.

    Subsection 5.2.2 Performing chance experiments in different situations

    Activity 5.2.2.

    \(\textbf{Work in groups}\)
    Suppose the bag contains:
    • \(4\) red counters
    • \(3\) blue counters
    • \(2\) green counters
    • \(1\) yellow counter
    This makes a total of \(10\) counters.
    1. Before drawing, predict how many times you think each color will appear if you draw 20 times, replacing the counter back into the bag after each draw.
    2. Perform the experiment by drawing a counter from the bag, recording its color, and putting it back before drawing again. Repeat \(20\) times and fill in the table below.
    3. Repeat the experiment by drawing counters 15 and 30 times.
    4. Analyze:
      • Which color appeared the most?
      • Which color appeared the least?
    5. Share and compare your findings with other groups.
    \(\textbf{Key Takeaway}\)
    • \(\textbf{Chance Experiment}\) is an activity or situation where the outcome is not certain. You dont know what will happen until you try it.
    • \(\textbf{Experiment}\text{:}\) A process that leads to one or more outcomes (e.g., rolling a die)
    • \(\textbf{trial}\text{:}\) One performance of the experiment (e.g., one roll of the die)
    • \(\textbf{outcome}\text{:}\) The result of a chance experiment.
    • \(\textbf{Probability}\text{:}\) A number that tells how likely an event is to happen, usually between \(0\) and \(1\text{.}\)
      • \(0\) means it cannot happen
      • \(1\) means it is certain to happen
      • A probability of \(0.5\) or \((50)\)% means its equally likely to happen or not

    Example 5.2.5.

    Akello has four different T-shirts in her drawer: navy, grey, white, and red. She chooses one T-shirt randomly. What are the possible colours of the T-shirt Akello picked?
    Solution.
    The possible colours of the T-shirt Akello picked are: navy, grey, white, red.

    Example 5.2.6.

    A basket contains \(4\) different fruits: apple, banana, peach, and pineapple. One fruit is randomly chosen from the basket.
    List the sample space of the possible types of fruit that could be chosen.
    Solution.
    The sample space of the possible types of fruit is:
    Sample Space = {apple, banana, peach, pineapple}

    Subsection 5.2.3 Writing experimental probability outcomes in different situations

    Activity 5.2.3.

    Work in groups
    Given a die shown below.
    1. Toss a die \(20\) times.
    2. How many times does each number from \(1\) to \(6\) appear out of \(20\) tosses of a die? Record in the table below.
    3. Write how many times each number on the die shows up as a fraction of the total number of times you rolled the die.
    4. Discuss and share with other groups.
    \(\textbf{Key Takeaway}\)
    • \(\textbf{Experimental probability outcome}\) refers to the result or observation of an event that occurs during an experiment or trial when calculating experimental probability.
    • \(\textbf{Experimental probability}\) refers to the probability of an event happening based on the results of an actual experiment or trial.
      Formula for Experimental Probability:
      \begin{align*} \textbf{P(E)} = \amp \frac{\textbf{Number of outcomes}}{\textbf{Total number of trials}} \end{align*}
      Where;
      • P(E) = Experimental probability of event
      • Number of outcomes = How many times the event you are interested in occurs
      • Total number of trials = The number of times the experiment is repeated

    Example 5.2.8.

    In a bag of marbles, there are red, blue, and green marbles. You pick a marble, note its color, and put it back. You do this \(30\) times. You pick a red marble \(12\) times. What is the experimental probability of picking a red marble?
    Solution.
    Total Number of times a red marble was picked = 12
    Total number of picks = \(30\)
    To calculate the experimental probability of picking a red marble, we use the formula:
    \begin{align*} \textbf{P(Red)} = \amp \frac{\textbf{Number of times a red marble was picked}}{\textbf{Total number of picks}}\\ = \amp \frac{12}{30}\\ = \amp \frac{2}{5}\\ = \amp 0.4 \end{align*}
    Therefore, the experimental probability of picking a red marble is \(0.4\) or \(40\)%.

    Example 5.2.9.

    Nanjala rolled a number cube \(25\) times and got an even number \(11\) times. Predict how many times she might get an even number if she rolls the cube \(100\) times.
    Solution.
    From the experiment:
    Nanjala rolled the number cube \(25\) times.
    She got an even number \(11\) times.
    So, the experimental probability of getting an even number is:
    \begin{align*} \textbf{P(Even)} = \amp \frac{\textbf{Number of even outcomes}}{\textbf{Total number of rolls}}\\ = \amp \frac{11}{25} \end{align*}
    To predict how many times Nanjala might get an even number in \(100\) rolls, we multiply the experimental probability by \(100\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} \textbf{Predicted number of even outcomes} = \amp \textbf{P(Even)} \times 100\\ = \amp \frac{11}{25} \times 100\\ = \amp 44 \end{align*}
    Therefore, Nanjala might get an even number \(44\) times if she rolls the number cube \(100\) times.

    Subsection 5.2.4 Expressing probability outcomes in fractions in different situations

    Activity 5.2.4.

    \(\textbf{Work in groups}\)
    In a bag, there are \(10\) marbles of different colors:
    • \(4\) red marbles
    • \(3\) blue marbles
    • \(2\) green marbles
    • \(1\) yellow marble
    A student picks one marble at random from the bag.
    1. Identify the total number of possible outcomes.
    2. Find number of times you pick a marble, probability of picking each color, express it as a fraction and fill in your results in the table below
    3. Discuss your results with other groups.
    \(\textbf{Key Takeaway}\)
    • \(\textbf{Expressing probability outcomes in fractions}\) means representing the likelihood of an event happening as a fraction.
      Where;
      • The numerator represents the number of favorable outcomes.
      • The denominator represents the total number of possible outcomes.

    Example 5.2.11.

    Akinyi tossed a fair coin \(30\) times and noted the result of each flip. She recorded whether the coin landed on heads or tails for every trial.
    The results of her experiments are shown in the table below.
    Find the probability of getting a;
    1. Head
    2. Tail
    Solution.
    1. Number of tosses (total possible outcomes) = \(10+20 = 30\)
      The probability of getting a head (H) is the ratio of the number of heads to the total number of tosses.
      \begin{align*} \textbf{P(Head)} = \amp \frac{\textbf{Number of heads}}{\textbf{Total number of tosses}}\\ = \amp \frac{10}{30}\\ = \amp \frac{1}{3} \end{align*}
      The probability of getting a Head is \(\frac{1}{3}\)
    2. The probability of getting a tail (T) is the ratio of the number of tails to the total number of tosses.
      \begin{align*} \textbf{P(Tail)} = \amp \frac{\textbf{Number of tails}}{\textbf{Total number of tosses}}\\ = \amp \frac{20}{30}\\ = \amp \frac{2}{3} \end{align*}
      The probability of getting a Tail is \(\frac{2}{3}\)

    Example 5.2.13.

    Mr. Indimuli puts \(20\) letters in a bag. The letters include \(6\)Fs, \(5\)Ds, \(4\)Cs, \(3\)Bs and \(2\)As. He will randomly select a letter out of the bag to determine your grade. Find the following probabilities. Give an answer as a fraction.
    1. Find the probability of getting an F.
    2. Find the probability of passing.
    3. Calculate the probability of getting an A or B.
    Solution.
    Total numbers of letters: \(6+5+4+3+2 = 20\)
    1. Number of \(\textbf{F's} = 6\)
      To calculate the probability of getting an F:
      \begin{align*} \textbf{P(F)} = \amp \frac{\textbf{Number of F's}}{\textbf{Total number of letters}}\\ = \amp \frac{6}{20}\\ = \amp \frac{3}{10} \end{align*}
      Therefore, the probability of getting an F is \(\frac{3}{10}\)
    2. Probability of passing: P(A,B,C,D).
      Number of As = \(2\text{,}\) Bs = \(3\) , Cs = \(4\text{,}\) Ds = \(5\)
      Total for A,B,C,D = \(5+4+3+2 = 14\)
      \begin{align*} \textbf{P(Passing)} = \amp \frac{\textbf{Number of A's + B'S + C'S + D'S}}{\textbf{Total number of letters}}\\ = \amp \frac{14}{20}\\ = \amp \frac{7}{10} \end{align*}
      The probability of passing a grade is \(\frac{7}{10}\)
    3. To calculate P(A,B):
      Total for As and Bs ; \(2+3 = 5\)
      \begin{align*} \textbf{P(A or B)} = \amp \frac{\textbf{Number of A's + Number of B'S }}{\textbf{Total number of letters}}\\ = \amp \frac{5}{20}\\ = \amp \frac{1}{4} \end{align*}
      The Probability of Getting an A or B is \(\frac{1}{4}\)

    Subsection 5.2.5 Expressing probability outcomes in decimals in different situations

    Activity 5.2.5.

    \(\textbf{Work in groups}\)
    Materials Needed:
    • Small colored objects (like beads or buttons) in 3 colors to represent watermelons, bananas, and mangoes.
      • E.g., green = watermelon, yellow = banana, orange = mango.
    • Small paper bags or containers
    • Worksheets or notebooks for recording answers
    • Calculators (optional)
    1. Place 7 green, 5 yellow, and 3 orange items into a bag to represent watermelons, bananas, and mangoes respectively, for a total of 15 fruits.
    2. Each student draws one item from the bag without looking, records the color (fruit), then returns it to the bag, repeating this process 10 times.
    3. Calculate:
      • Count how many times they picked each color (fruit).
      • Then calculate the experimental probability for each fruit:
      • Write your answer in decimal form.
    4. Discuss and share your answer with other groups.
    \(\textbf{Key Takeaway}\)
    • \(\textbf{Expressing probability outcomes in decimals}\) means representing the likelihood of events using numbers between 0 and 1 (eg 0.25), where:
      • 0 represents an impossible event (0% chance).
      • 1 represents a certain event (100% chance).
      • Any number between 0 and 1 (like 0.25, 0.5, 0.75) shows how likely the event is:
        • 0.5 = 50% chance (like flipping a fair coin and getting heads).
        • 0.25 = 25% chance.
        • 0.75 = 75% chance.
      • Probabilities always add up to 1.0.

    Example 5.2.14.

    A bag contains seven watermelons, five bananas and three mangoes. What is the probability that a fruit selected at random is:
    1. A watermelon?
    2. A banana?
    3. A mango?
    Express the answer as a decimal.
    Solution.
    Watermelons = \(7\)
    Bananas = \(5\)
    Mangoes = \(3\)
    Total number of fruits \(7+5+3 = 15\)
    1. Calculating the probability of selecting a watermelon, we use formula:
      \begin{align*} \textbf{P(Watermelon)} = \amp \frac{\textbf{Number of possible outcomes of a watermelon}}{\textbf{Total number of fruits}}\\ = \amp \frac{7}{15}\\ = \amp 0.4667 \end{align*}
      Therefore, the probability of selcting a watermelon is \(0.4667\)
    2. Calculating the probability of selecting a banana, we use formula:
      \begin{align*} \textbf{P(Banana)} = \amp \frac{\textbf{Number of possible outcomes of a banana}}{\textbf{Total number of fruits}}\\ = \amp \frac{5}{15}\\ = \amp \frac{1}{3}\\ = \amp 0.3333 \end{align*}
      Therefore, the probability of selcting a banana is \(0.3333\)
    3. Calculating the probability of selecting a mango, we use formula:
      \begin{align*} \textbf{P(Mango)} = \amp \frac{\textbf{Number of possible outcomes of a mango}}{\textbf{Total number of fruits}}\\ = \amp \frac{3}{15}\\ = \amp \frac{1}{5}\\ = \amp 0.2 \end{align*}
      Therefore, the probability of selcting a mango is \(0.2\)

    Example 5.2.15.

    In a school with \(50\) students, \(12\) students have a bicycle. What is the probability that a student picked at random owns a bicycle? Write the answer in decimal form.
    Solution.
    The total number of students is: \(50\)
    The number of students who own a bicycle is: \(12\)
    Using the probability formula:
    \begin{align*} \textbf{Probability} = \amp \frac{\textbf{Number of favorable outcomes}}{\textbf{Total number of outcomes}}\\ = \amp \frac{12}{50}\\ = \amp 0.24 \end{align*}
    So, the probability that a randomly selected student owns a bicycle is \(0.24\text{.}\)

    Subsection 5.2.6 Expressing probability outcomes in percentages in different situations

    Activity 5.2.6.

    \(\textbf{Work in groups}\)
    1. In a class of \(30\) students, each learner should mention their favorite pet from the following options: Dog, Cat, Rabbit and Parrot.
    2. Copy and fill in the table below with the class results.
    3. Convert each probability to a percentage.
    4. Discuss and share with other groups
    \(\textbf{Key Takeaway}\)
    • Probability can be expressed as a percentage to show how likely an event is to occur. A probability percentage tells us the chance of an event happening out of 100.
      • A probability of \(0\)% means the event cannot happen.
      • An event with a probability of \(50\)% has an equal chance of occurring or not occurring.
      • A probability of \(100\)% means the event will definitely happen.
      • The total probability of all possible outcomes always adds up to \(100\)%

    Example 5.2.17.

    Suppose you roll a die \(12\) times and the number \(4\) appears \(3\) times. Determine the probability of rolling a \(4\) in the next roll as a percentage.
    Solution.
    Number of trials = \(12\)
    Number of times \(4\) appeared = \(3\)
    Using the formula:
    \begin{align*} \textbf{Probability of getting a 4} = \amp \frac{\textbf{Number of times 4 appears}}{\textbf{Total trials}}\\ = \amp \frac{3}{12} \end{align*}
    Convert to a percentage:
    \begin{align*} = \amp \frac{3}{12} \times 100\%\\ = \amp 25\% \end{align*}

    Example 5.2.18.

    You interviewed \(10\) classmates and asked them to name their favorite subject from the following options: Science, Maths, English, and Social Studies. The results were:
    • \(3\) classmates prefer Science
    • \(2\) classmates prefer Maths
    • \(4\) classmates prefer English
    • \(1\) classmate prefers Social Studies
    1. What is the probability, as a percentage, that a learner prefers English?
    2. What is the probability, as a percentage, that a learner prefers Science?
    Solution.
    1. Number of learners who prefer English = \(4\)
      Total number of learners = \(10\)
      \begin{align*} \textbf{Probability} = \amp \frac{\textbf{Number of learners who prefer English}}{\textbf{Total number of learners}}\\ = \amp \frac{4}{10} \end{align*}
      Convert to percentage:
      \begin{align*} = \amp \frac{4}{10} \times 100\%\\ = \amp 40\% \end{align*}
      So, the probability that a learner prefers English is \(40\%\text{.}\)
    2. Number of learners who prefer Science = \(3\)
      Total number of learners = \(10\)
      \begin{align*} \textbf{Probability} = \amp \frac{\textbf{Number of learners who prefer Science}}{\textbf{Total number of learners}}\\ = \amp \frac{3}{10} \end{align*}
      Convert to percentage:
      \begin{align*} = \amp \frac{3}{10} \times 100\%\\ = \amp 30\% \end{align*}
      So, the probability that a learner prefers Science is \(30\%\text{.}\)
