    Section 1.2 Fractions

    A fraction is a number that represents a part of a whole. Fractions are made up of two numbers: a numerator and a denominator. The numerator is the number on top and the denominator is the number on the bottom. The numerator represents the number of parts you have and the denominator represents the total number of parts in the whole.

    Subsection 1.2.1 Combined operations involving addition and subtraction.

    To perform combined operations involving addition and subtraction of fractions, follow the steps below:
    \(\textbf{Step 1}\)
    Convert mixed numbers to improper fractions i.e for \(5 \frac{1}{3}\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} 5 \frac{1}{3} =\amp \frac{3 \times 5 + 1}{3} \\ =\amp \frac{16}{3} \end{align*}
    \(\textbf{Step 2}\)
    Find the LCM of the denominators if the fractions have different denominators. The LCM is the smallest number that both denominators can divide into.
    \(\textbf{Step 3}\)
    Rewrite the fractions so that it has the same denominator i.e.
    \(\frac{2}{3} + \frac{1}{4}\) \(= \frac{8+3}{12}\)
    \(\textbf{Step 4}\)
    Once the fractions have the same denominator, you can perform the addition or subtraction of the fractions e.g., for addition case:
    \begin{align*} \frac{2}{3} + \frac{1}{4} =\amp \frac{8+3}{12} \\ =\amp \frac{11}{12} \end{align*}
    For subtraction case ,then:
    \begin{align*} \frac{2}{3} - \frac{1}{4} =\amp \frac{8-3}{12} \\ =\amp \frac{5}{12} \end{align*}
    \(\textbf{Step 5}\)
    Simplify the result if the numerator and denominator have any common factors, by dividing both by their GCD.

    Activity 1.2.1.

    Cheruto is helping a chef prepare a recipe for a grade \(8\) end year celebration cake. The ingredients of the recipe are:
    Flour: \(\frac{3}{4}\) of a cup
    Sugar: \(\frac{2}{3}\) of a cup
    Butter: \(\frac{1}{2}\) of a cup
    Milk: \(\frac{5}{6}\) of a cup
    1. How much amount of ingredients is needed to fill the measuring cups of each of the \(4\) ingredients.
    2. Cheruto realizes they accidentally used an additional \(\frac{1}{6}\) cup of butter. How much butter did Cheruto and the chef used in total?
    3.What is the total amount of ingredients needed for the cake?

    Example 1.2.1.

    Oduor a grade \(8\) parent wanted to sew a skirt for her daughter. She bought \(2 \frac{2}{3}\) m of white cloth and \(2 \frac{1}{4}\) m of black cloth. The tailor used \(3\frac{1}{3}\) m of the cloths to make the skirt. Calculate the total length of the cloths that remained.
    Solution.
    From the above information, lets write an expression.
    \(2 \frac{2}{3}\) m + \(2 \frac{1}{4}\) m - \(3\frac{1}{3}\) m
    Then, convert mixed numbers to improper fractions.
    \(\frac{8}{3}\) m + \(\frac{9}{4}\) m - \(\frac{10}{3}\) m
    Applying order of operations, we perform addition:
    \(\frac{8}{3}\) m + \(\frac{9}{4}\) m
    LCM of \(3\) and \(4\) is \(12\) hence
    \begin{align*} \frac{8}{3} \text{m} + \frac{9}{4} \text{m} =\amp \frac{32+27}{12} \text{m} \\ =\amp \frac{59}{12} \text{m} \end{align*}
    Next we rewrite the expression and perform subtraction.
    \(\frac{59}{12}\) m - \(\frac{10}{3} \) m
    Since the LCM of \(12\) and \(3\) is \(12\) then,
    \begin{align*} \frac{59}{12} \text{m} - \frac{10}{3} \text{m} =\amp \frac{59-40}{12} \text{m}\\ =\amp \frac{19}{12} \text{m} \end{align*}
    The fraction \(\frac{19}{12}\) m cannot be simplified further therefore we convert the improper fraction to a mixed number.
    \(= 1\frac{7}{12}\) m

    Example 1.2.2.

    Work out the following fraction:
    \(\frac{2}{3} + \frac{2}{7} - \frac{1}{2}\)
    Solution.
    First, find the LCM of the \(3\) denominators, that is LCM of \(3,7\) and \(2\) which is \(42\text{.}\)
    Then using the LCM perform the operations starting from addition followed by subtraction.
    \begin{align*} \frac{2}{3} + \frac{2}{7} - \frac{1}{2} =\amp \frac{12+14-21}{42}\\ \amp \\ \frac{12+14-21}{42} =\amp \frac{26-21}{42}\\ \amp \\ =\amp \frac{5}{42} \end{align*}

    Subsection 1.2.2 Combined operations involving multiplication and division.

    Following order of operations, we perform division first then multiplication .
    To perform division of Fractions:
    • Multiply the first fraction by the reciprocal of the second fraction (flip the numerator and denominator of the second fraction).
    • Example :Given \(\frac{1}{9} \div \frac{1}{6}\text{,}\) then
    • \(\displaystyle \frac{1}{9} \div \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{9}\times \frac{6}{1}\)
    To perform multiplication of Fractions:
    Multiply the numerators together and the denominators together. Simplify the result if possible.
    Example:
    \begin{align*} \frac{2}{3} \times \frac{3}{5} =\amp\frac{2 \times3}{3 \times 5} \\ =\amp \frac{6}{15} \end{align*}
    Simplify \(\frac{6}{15}\) by dividing the denominator and the numerator by \(3\) we get: \(\frac{2}{5}\text{.}\)

    Example 1.2.4.

    Work out the following:
    1. \(\displaystyle \frac{2}{3} \div \frac{4}{5} \times \frac{6}{7}\)
    2. \(\displaystyle \frac{3}{4} \times \frac{2}{5} \div \frac{1}{2}\)
    Solution.
    a) Given \(\frac{2}{3} \div \frac{4}{5} \times \frac{6}{7}\) we perform division first.
    Division of fractions is the same as multiplying by the reciprocal i.e.
    \begin{align*} \frac{2}{3} \div \frac{4}{5} = \amp \frac{2}{3} \times \frac{5}{4}\\ \frac{2}{3} \times \frac{5}{4} =\amp \frac{2 \times 5}{3 \times4}\\ =\amp \frac{10}{12} \end{align*}
    Simplify \(\frac{10}{12}\) by dividing numerator and denominator by \(2\text{:}\)
    \(= \frac{5}{6}\)
    Now multiply the result by \(\frac{6}{7}\)​:
    \begin{align*} \frac{5}{6} \times \frac{6}{7} =\amp \frac{5 \times 6}{6 \times 7} \\ =\amp \frac{30}{42} \end{align*}
    Simplify \(\frac{30}{42}\) by dividing numerator and denominator by \(6\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} \frac{30}{42} \amp= \frac{5}{7} \end{align*}
    Therefore,
    \begin{align*} \frac{2}{3} \div \frac{4}{5} \times \frac{6}{7} =\amp \frac{5}{7} \end{align*}
    b) For \(\frac{3}{4} \times \frac{2}{5} \div \frac{1}{2}\)
    Divide \(\frac{2}{5}\) by \(\frac{1}{2}\) first.
    \begin{align*} \frac{2}{5} \div \frac{1}{2} =\amp \frac{2}{5} \times \frac{2}{1} = \frac{2 \times 2}{5 \times 1} \\ =\amp \frac{4}{5} \end{align*}
    Rewriting the expression we have: \(\frac{3}{4} \times \frac{4}{5}\text{.}\)
    Performing multiplication:
    \begin{align*} \frac{3}{4} \times \frac{4}{5} =\amp \frac{3 \times4}{4 \times 5} \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} \frac{3 \times {\cancel{4}}}{\cance{4}\times 5}=\amp \frac{3}{5} \end{align*}
    Hence
    \begin{align*} \frac{3}{4} \times \frac{2}{5} \div \frac{1}{2} =\amp \frac{3}{5} \end{align*}

    Example 1.2.5.

    A farmer has \(4\frac{1}{2}\) acres of land. He plans to plant corn on \(2\frac{1}{4}\) of the land, and then divide that portion into plots of \(\frac{3}{4z}\) acre each. How many plots of corn can the farmer plant?
    Solution.
    First, convert mixed numbers to improper fractions.
    \begin{align*} 4\frac{1}{2} =\amp \frac{9}{2} \\ 2\frac{1}{4} =\amp \frac{9}{4} \end{align*}
    To find the land for corn, multiply the total fraction of the land by the portion of the land for planting corn.
    Land for corn
    \begin{align*} = \frac{9}{2} \times \frac{9}{4} =\amp \frac{9 \times 9}{8} \\ =\amp \frac{81}{8} \end{align*}
    Next, divide the land for corn by the size of each plot to find the number of plots he can plant.
    Number of plots \(= \frac{81}{8} \div \frac{3}{4}\)
    Since dividing by a fraction is the same as multiplying by its reciprocal, then:
    \(= \frac{81}{8} \div \frac{3}{4} = \frac{81}{8} \times \frac{4}{3}\)
    Number of plots \(\frac{81}{\cancel{8}} \times \frac{\cancel{4}}{3} = \frac{81 \times 1}{2 \times 3}\)
    \(\frac{81 \times 1}{2 \times 3} = \frac{81}{6} \)
    Simplify the fraction \(\frac{81}{6} \) by dividing through by \(3\) .
    \(\frac{81}{6} = \frac{27}{3}\)
    Therefore, number of plots \(= 13\frac{1}{2}\)

    Subsection 1.2.3 Combined operations involving three operations

    When you are required to perform combined operations involving three operations, carry out operations according to their order.
    Order of operations can be:
    1. Division \(\rightarrow\) Addition \(\rightarrow\) Subtraction
    2. Multiplication \(\rightarrow\) Addition \(\rightarrow\) Subtraction
    3. Division \(\rightarrow\) Multiplication \(\rightarrow\) Subtraction
    4. Division \(\rightarrow\) multiplication \(\rightarrow\) Subtraction

    Activity 1.2.3.

    In Masomo college, \(\frac{2}{5}\) of the population study agriculture and \(\frac{1}{4}\) of the population study business.The rest of the population study artisan courses. In \(2024\text{,}\) \(\frac{1}{2}\) of the agriculture students dropped out of the course to study mathematics, \(\frac{1}{3}\) of the business students went for attachment and \(\frac{1}{5}\) of the artisan students learned a new skill.
      1. Working in groups of \(5\text{,}\) write an expression to calculate the fraction of the student population who pursued artisan courses?
      2. What fraction of the student population pursued artisan courses? \(\textbf{Hint:} {\color{blue}\text{Total population = 1}}\)
    2. What fraction of agriculture students dropped agriculture to do mathematics?
    3. What fraction of business students went for attachment?
    4. What fraction of the artisan students learned a new skill?
    5. What fraction of the total population dropped agriculture, went for attachment, or learned a new skill? \(\textbf{Hint:} {\color{blue}\text{ Add the fractions from questions b, c, and d}}\)

    Example 1.2.6.

    Work out: \(\frac{5}{6} \times \frac{3}{10}+ \frac{2}{3} \div \frac{4}{5}\)
    Solution.
    To work out \(\frac{5}{6} \times \frac{3}{10}+ \frac{2}{3} \div \frac{4}{5} \text{,}\) follow the order:
    Division \(\rightarrow\) Multiplication \(\rightarrow\) Addition
    Carry out division by multiplying \(\frac{2}{3}\) by the reciprocal of \(\frac{4}{5}\text{,}\) which is \(\frac{5}{4}\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} =\amp \frac{5}{6} \times \frac{3}{10}+ \frac{2}{3} \times \frac{5}{4} \\ \frac{\cancel{2}^1}{3} \times \frac{5}{\cancel{4}_2} =\amp \frac{1 \times 5}{3 \times 2}\\ =\amp \frac{5}{6} \end{align*}
    Carry out multiplication: \(\frac{5}{6} \times \frac{3}{10} + \frac{5}{6}\)
    \begin{align*} \frac{\cancel{5}^1}{\cancel{6}_2} \times \frac{\cancel{3}^1}{\cancel{10}_2} =\amp \frac{1 \times 1}{2 \times 2}\\ =\amp \frac{1}{4} \end{align*}
    Carry out addition: \(\frac{1}{4} + \frac{5}{6}\text{.}\) The LCM of \(4\) and \(6\) is \(24\)
    \begin{align*} \frac{1}{4} + \frac{5}{6} =\amp \frac{6+20}{24} \\ =\amp \frac{26}{24} \end{align*}
    Simplify \(\frac{26}{24}\) by dividing both the numerator and the denominator by \(2\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} \frac{26}{24} =\amp \frac{13}{12} \end{align*}
    \(\frac{13}{12}\) written in an improper fraction is \(1\frac{1}{12}\)

    Subsection 1.2.4 Combined operations involving all operations

    To perform combined operations involving addition, subtraction, multiplication and division, follow this order:
    Division \(\rightarrow\) Multiplication \(\rightarrow\) Addition \(\rightarrow\) Subtraction

    Activity 1.2.4.

    1. In groups of \(5\text{,}\) write down numbers from \(2\) to \(15\) in ascendinding order \((2,...,15)\text{.}\)Label this as the first list.
    2. Rearrange the numbers from \(15\) to \(2\) in descending order \((15, 14, 13, ..., 2)\text{.}\) Label this as the Second List.
    3. In turns, each group member to create a fraction by:
      1. Using the first number from the First List as the numerator.
      2. Using the first number from the Second List as the denominator.
      3. Repeat the process until all numbers are used.
    4. Use the fractions you have formed to write \(2\) expressions involving multiplication, division, addition and subtraction e.g \(\frac{2}{15} \times \frac{3}{14} + \frac{5}{12} \div \frac{10}{7} - \frac{7}{10}\)
    5. Work out the value of each expression you have written.

    Example 1.2.8.

    Work out: \(\frac{7}{12} \div \frac{1}{9} - \frac{4}{7} + \frac{6}{7} \times \frac{1}{6}\)
    Solution.
    To work out \(\frac{7}{12} \div \frac{1}{9} - \frac{4}{7} + \frac{6}{7} \times \frac{1}{6}\) follow this order:
    Division \(\rightarrow\) Multiplication \(\rightarrow\) Addition \(\rightarrow\) Subtraction
    Carry out division by multiplying \(\frac{7}{12}\) by the reciprocal of \(\frac{1}{9}\text{,}\) which is \(\frac{9}{1}\)
    \begin{align*} =\amp \frac{7}{\cancel{12}_4} \times \frac{\cancel{9}^3}{1} - \frac{4}{7}+ \frac{6}{7} \times \frac{1}{6} \\ \amp \\ =\amp \frac{7 \times 3}{4 \times 1} - \frac{4}{7}+ \frac{6}{7} \times \frac{1}{6} \\ \amp \\ =\amp \frac{21}{4} - \frac{4}{7}+ \frac{6}{7} \times \frac{1}{6} \end{align*}
    Carry out multiplication:
    \begin{align*} \frac{21}{4} - \frac{4}{7} + \frac{\cancel{6}^1}{7} \times \frac{1}{\cancel{6}_1} =\amp \frac{21}{4} - \frac{4}{7} + \frac{1 \times 1}{7 \times 1}\\ \amp \\ =\amp \frac{21}{4} - \frac{4}{7} + \frac{1}{7} \end{align*}
    Carry out addition. The LCM of \(4\) and \(7\) is \(28\)
    \begin{align*} \frac{21}{4} - \frac{4}{7} + \frac{1}{7}=\amp \frac{21}{4} + \frac{1}{7} - \frac{4}{7}\\ \amp \\ \frac{21}{24} + \frac{1}{7} =\amp \frac{147 +4}{28}\\ \amp \\ =\amp \frac{151}{28} \end{align*}
    Carry out subtraction. The LCM of \(28\) and \(7\) is \(28\)
    \begin{align*} \frac{151}{28} - \frac{4}{7} =\amp \frac{151-16}{28}\\ \amp \\ =\amp \frac{135}{28} \end{align*}
    Convert \(\frac{135}{28} \)into a mixed fraction.
    \begin{align*} =\amp 4\frac{23}{28}. \end{align*}

    Subsection 1.2.5 Combined operations involving brackets

    To work out combined operations of fractions involving brackets we:
    1. Look at the expression : \((\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{3} )\times \frac{3}{4} - \frac{1}{6}\) and identify the operations inside and outside the brackets.
    2. Following the order of operations, solve the operations inside the brackets first i.e . \((\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{3})\)
    3. Find the LCM of the denominators, \(2\) and \(3\) which is \(6\text{.}\) Then add the fractions:
      \begin{align*} \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{3} =\amp\frac{3+2}{6} \\ \amp \\ =\amp \frac{5}{6} \end{align*}
    4. After simplifying the operations inside the brackets, perform any other operation involved ensuring to follow order.
      \begin{align*} \frac{5}{6} \times \frac{3}{4} -\frac{1}{6} \\ \amp \\ \frac{5}{6} \times \frac{3}{4}=\amp \frac{5 \times 3}{6 \times 4} \\ \amp \\ =\amp \frac{15}{24}\\ \amp \\ \frac{15}{24} - \frac{1}{6} =\amp \frac{15-4}{24} \\ \amp \\ =\amp\frac{11}{24} \end{align*}
    5. Reduce the final fraction to its simplest form if possible. Since \(\frac{11}{24}\) is already in its simplest form we leave the answer as \(\frac{11}{24}\text{.}\)

    Example 1.2.9.

    Work out: \(\frac{1}{3}(\frac{3}{4} \div \frac{1}{2}) \times 1\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2}- \frac{1}{8}\)
    Solution.
    1. Work out the brackets first
    \(\frac{1}{3}(\frac{3}{4} \div \frac{1}{2})\)
    \(\frac{3}{4} \div \frac{1}{2} = \frac{3}{4} \times \frac{2}{1} = \frac{3}{2}\)
    \(\frac{1}{3}(\frac{3}{2})\) is the same as \(\frac{1}{3} \times \frac{3}{2}\)
    2. Rewriting the expression we have: \(\frac{1}{3} \times \frac{3}{2} \times 1\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{8}\)
    3. Working out multiplication: \(\frac{1}{3} \times \frac{3}{2} \times 1\frac{1}{2} \)
    Convert \(1\frac{1}{2}\) into an improper fraction that is: \(\frac{2 \times 1 + 1}{2} = \frac{3}{2}\)
    Multiplying \(\frac{1}{\cancel{3}} \times \frac{\cancel{3}}{2} \times \frac{3}{2} = \frac{1 \times 1 \times3}{1 \times 2 \times 2}\)
    Hence, \(\frac{1}{3} \times \frac{3}{2} \times 1\frac{1}{2} \) \(= \frac{3}{4}\) and the expression becomes \(\frac{3}{4}+ \frac{1}{2}- \frac{1}{8}\)
    4. Working out addition:
    \begin{align*} \frac{3}{4}+\frac{1}{2} =\amp \frac{3+2}{4} \\ =\amp \frac{5}{4} \end{align*}
    5. Working out subtraction:
    \begin{align*} \frac{5}{4} - \frac{1}{8} =\amp \frac{10-1}{8}\\ =\amp \frac{2}{8} \end{align*}
    6. Simplify \(\frac{2}{8}\) by dividing through by \(2\text{.}\)
    Therefore, \(\frac{1}{3}(\frac{3}{4} \div \frac{1}{2}) \times 1\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2}- \frac{1}{8} = \frac{1}{4}\)

    1. Measuring quantities during cooking, construction,...

    Example 1.2.10.

    Cheptoo spends \(\frac{1}{8}\) of her salary on food, \(\frac{1}{2}\) on rent, \(\frac{1}{2}\) of the remainder on school fees and saves the rest. If she save sh \(4,200\text{,}\) calculate:
    1. The fraction of the salary Cheptoo saved.
    2. The amount of money she spent on food
    3. The amount of money she spent on rent.
    4. The amount of money she spent on shool fees.
    Solution.
    1. Let Cheptoo’s salary be a whole number represented by \(1\)
      Fraction of the remaining amount of money after spending on food and rent is:
      Fraction of food \(= \frac{1}{8}\)
      Fraction of rent \(= \frac{1}{2}\)
      \begin{align*} 1-(\frac{1}{8} + \frac{1}{2}) \amp \\ \frac{1}{8} + \frac{1}{2} =\amp \frac{1+4}{8}\\ =\amp \frac{5}{8}\\ 1- \frac{5}{8} =\amp \frac{1}{1} - \frac{5}{8}\\ \frac{8-5}{8} =\amp \frac{3}{8} \end{align*}
      Fraction of school fees:
      \begin{align*} \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{3}{8}=\amp \frac{1 \times 3}{2 \times 8}\\ \amp \\ =\amp \frac{3}{16} \end{align*}
      Fraction of the money she spent on food,school fees and rent:
      Food \(= \frac{1}{8}\)
      Rent \(= \frac{1}{2}\)
      School fees \(= \frac{3}{16}\)
      \begin{align*} \frac{1}{8} + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{3}{16} =\amp \frac{2+8+3}{16} \\ \amp \\ =\amp \frac{13}{16} \end{align*}
      Fraction of money she saved is:
      \begin{align*} \frac{1}{1} - \frac{13}{16} =\amp \frac{16 - 13}{16}\\ \amp \\ =\amp \frac{3}{16} \end{align*}
    2. The amount of money she spent on food, rent and school fees:
      Since she saved sh \(4 200\) and the fraction she saved is \(\frac{3}{16}\text{,}\) it means that \(\frac{3}{16}\) represents sh \(4 200\)
      The total salary represented by \(1\) is given by:
      \begin{align*} 1 \times 4 200 \div \frac{3}{16} =\amp 1 \times 4 200 \times \frac{16}{3}\\ \amp \\ =\amp 4200 \times \frac{16}{3} \end{align*}
      1. Amount spent on food = \(\frac{1}{8}\) of the total salary
        \begin{align*} \frac{1}{8} \times 4200 \times \frac{16}{3} =\amp \frac{1}{\cancel{8}_1} \times \frac{\cancel{16}^2}{\cancel{3}_1} \times \cancel{4200}^{1400} \\ \amp \\ =\amp \text{ sh} \quad 2800 \end{align*}
      2. Amount spent on rent = \(\frac{1}{2}\) of the total salary
        \begin{align*} \frac{1}{2} \times 4200 \times \frac{16}{3} =\amp \frac{1}{\cancel{2}_1} \times \frac{\cancel{16}^8}{\cancel{3}_1} \times \cancel{4200}^{1400} \\ \amp \\ =\amp \text{ sh} \quad 11 200 \end{align*}
      3. Amount spent on school fees = \(\frac{3}{16}\) of the total salary
        But we already know that \(\frac{3}{16}\) represents sh \(4 200\)
        Hence amount spent on school fees \(= \) sh \(4 200\)
