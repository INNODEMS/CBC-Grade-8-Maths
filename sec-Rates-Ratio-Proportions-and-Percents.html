    Section 1.5 Rates Ratio Proportions and Percents

    Rates, ratios, proportions, and percents are all ways of comparing quantities. They are used in many different situations, from cooking to sports to finance. Understanding how to work with rates, ratios, proportions, and percents is an important part of being able to solve problems in many different areas.

    Subsection 1.5.1 Rates

    A rate is a ratio that compares two quantities measured in different units. For example, if a car travels 100 miles in 2 hours, the rate is \(\frac{100 \, \text{miles}}{2 \, \text{hours}}\) \(= 50\) miles per hour.

    Subsubsection 1.5.1.1 Identifying Rates in Different Situations

    Activity 1.5.1. Work in Groups.
    1. Using a mobile phone, note the amount of data at the moment and the cost of the data plan.
    2. Make an attempt of surfing the internet for a few minutes and note the amount of data used.
    3. Check the balance of the data availabe and calculate the amount of data spent.
    4. Repeat steps 2 and 3 for two different service providers.
    5. Copy your findings in a table and calculate the rate of data usage for each service provider.
    6. Discuss and compare the different data rates with other groups

    Subsubsection 1.5.1.2 Working out Rates in Real Life Situations

    Activity 1.5.2.
    Work in Groups
    Consider a phone has a data plan of \(1\) GB for Kshs \(10\) for an hour.
    1. Find the cost of surfing the internet for \(30\) minutes.
    2. Discusss and share with other groups.
    \begin{gather*} \textbf{Key Takeway} \end{gather*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{A rate is a ratio that compares two quantities measured in different units.} \end{equation*}
    Example 1.5.2.
    A car travels \(100\) miles in \(2\) hours. Find the rate of the car.
    Solution.
    The rate of the car is \(100\) miles in \(2\) hours.
    To calculate the rate, divide the distance by the time taken.
    The rate of the car can be given by the formula:
    \begin{equation*} Rate = \frac{Distance}{Time} \end{equation*}
    Substituting the values of distance and time, we get:
    \begin{equation*} Rate = \frac{100 \, miles}{2 \, hours} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} Rate \, of \, speed = 50\, miles\, per\, hour \end{equation*}
    Example 1.5.3.
    A labourer digs \(10\) holes in \(5\) hours. Find the rate of digging holes.
    Solution.
    The rate of digging holes is \(10\) holes in \(5\) hours.
    To calculate the rate, divide the number of holes by the time taken.
    The rate of digging holes can be given by the formula:
    \begin{equation*} Rate = \frac{Number\, of\, Holes}{Time} \end{equation*}
    Substituting the values of number of holes and time, we get:
    \begin{equation*} Rate = \frac{10}{5} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} Rate = 2 \, holes\, per\, hour \end{equation*}
    Example 1.5.4.
    A family of \(4\) consumes \(20\) litres of milk in \(5\) days. Find the rate of consumption of milk.
    Solution.
    The rate of consumption of milk is \(20\) litres in \(5\) days.
    To calculate the rate, divide the amount of milk by the time taken.
    The rate of consumption of milk can be given by the formula:
    \begin{equation*} Rate = \frac{Amount\, of\, Milk}{Time} \end{equation*}
    Substituting the values of amount of milk and time, we get:
    \begin{equation*} Rate = \frac{20}{5} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} Rate = 4 \, litres\, per\, day \end{equation*}

    Subsection 1.5.2 Ratio

    A ratio is a comparison of two numbers. Ratios can be written in three ways: as a fraction, with a colon, or with the word "to."

    Subsubsection 1.5.2.1 Experessing Fractions as Ratios in Real-Life Situations.

    Activity 1.5.3.
    Work in Groups.
    1. Divide a strip of paper into \(8\) equal parts.
    2. Color three parts red and five parts blue.
    3. Cut out the eight parts.
    4. Find the fraction of the strips represented by red cut-outs.
    5. Find the fraction of the strips represented by blue cut-outs.
    6. Express the number of the red cut-outs as fractions to the number of the blue cut-outs.
    7. Find the ratio of red cut-outs to blue cut-outs.
    8. Discuss and share your results with other groups.
    \begin{align*} \amp \textbf{Learning Point} \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} \amp \text{Fraction} \, (\frac{a}{b})\, \text{can be expressed as a ratio} \, (a:b), \, i.e \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} \amp \frac{3}{8} = 3:8 \end{align*}
    Example 1.5.5.
    The number of boys in a class of \(50\) learners is \(30\text{.}\)
    1. Find the number of girls.
    2. Express the number of boys as a fraction to the number of girls.
    3. Find the ratio of the number of boys to the number of girls.
    Solution.
    1. The number of girls in the class is given as;
      \begin{equation*} \text{Total number of learners of a class} - \text{Number of boys in the class} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} 50 - 30 = 20 \end{equation*}
      girls present in class.
    2. To express the of boys to girls as fraction, we have to divide the number of boys to that of girls in the class
      Previously, we had boys = \(30\) and girls = \(20\text{.}\)
      \begin{equation*} \text{Boys as a fractions} = \frac{30}{20} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} = \frac{3}{2} \end{equation*}
    3. Expressing fractions to ratios we introduce colons instead of the fraction bar.
      \begin{equation*} \text{Ratio of boys to girls} = 3:2 \end{equation*}
    Example 1.5.6.
    A solution contains \(20\) grams of salt and \(100\) millilitres of water. Find the ratio of salt to water.
    Solution.
    To find the ratio of salt to water, we have to divide the amount of salt by the amount of water.
    Expressing fractions to ratios we introduce colons.
    \begin{equation*} \text{Fraction of salt to water} = \frac{20}{100} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = \frac{1}{5} \end{equation*}
    Therefore, the ratio of salt to water is
    \begin{equation*} \text{Ratio of salt to water} = 1:5\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Example 1.5.7.
    A solution consists of \(\frac{3}{5}\) of liquid A and the rest is liquid B.
    1. Express the liquid A as a fraction to liquid B in the mixture.
    2. Find the ratio A:B.
    Solution.
    To find the amount in solution B we substrat the amount of liquid A from the total amount of the solution.
    The total solution adds up to \(1\text{.}\)
    Therefore, the amount of liquid B is \(1 - \frac{3}{5} = \frac{2}{5}\text{.}\)
    To express the liquid A as a fraction to liquid B, we have to divide the amount of liquid A by the amount of liquid B.
    1. Now lets divide the amount of liquid A by the amount of liquid B.
      \begin{equation*} \frac{Liquid \, A}{Liquid \, B} = \frac{\frac{3}{5}}{\frac{2}{5}} \end{equation*}
      To solve for the fraction we invert the divisor and multiply.
      \begin{equation*} = (\frac{3}{5} \times \frac{5}{2}) \end{equation*}
      Therefore, the amount of liquid A as a fraction to liquid B is;
      \begin{equation*} Liquid \, A \text{as a fraction} = \frac{3}{2} \end{equation*}
    2. Expressing fractions to ratios we introduce colons to separate the numerator and the denominator of the fraction.
      \begin{equation*} \text{Ratio A:B} = 3:2 \end{equation*}

    Subsubsection 1.5.2.2 Comparing Two Ratios in Differnt Situations

    Activity 1.5.4.
    Work in Groups.
    (Use two strips of paper of equal lenghts in this activity)
    Consider the ratio \(2:3\) and \(4:5.\)
    1. For \(2:3\) divide one strip into five equal parts and colour the first two parts with green.
    2. For \(4:5\) divide one strip of paper into nine equal parts and colour the first four part with blue colour.
    3. Compare the lengths of the colored parts on each strip.
    4. Discuss and share with other groups.
    \begin{align*} \amp \textbf{Learning Point} \end{align*}
    \(\text{To compare rates,}\)
    • Express each ratio as a fraction.
    • Write the equivalenta form for each fraction such that the fraction have the same denominator.
    Activity 1.5.5.
    Work in Groups
    Consider the ratios \(3:4\) and \(5:7\)
    1. Write the two ratios as fractions.
    2. Express each fraction as equivalent fraction such that they have the same denominator.
    3. Identify the greater and lesser fraction.
    4. Discuss and share other groups.
    Example 1.5.8.
    Compare the rates \(3:5\) and \(7:8.\)
    Solution.
    First we convert the ratios to fractions
    \begin{align*} 3:5 = \amp \frac{3}{5} \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} 7:8 = \amp \frac{7}{8} \end{align*}
    Then we find the LCM of the fractions using the denominators.
    The LCM of \(5\) and \(8\) is \(40.\) Now to solve for the initial fraction, we multiply one fraction with the value of the denominator of the other fraction.
    \begin{align*} \frac{3}{5} = \amp \frac{3 \times 8}{5 \times 8} \text{,} \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} \frac{7}{8} = \amp \frac{7 \times 5}{8 \times 5} \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} = \amp \frac{24}{40}, \frac{35}{40} \end{align*}
    Thus,
    \begin{align*} 3:5 = \amp 24:40 \end{align*}
    and
    \begin{align*} 3:5 = \amp 35:40 \end{align*}
    Therefore, \(3:5\) is less than \(7:8\)

    Subsubsection 1.5.2.3 Comparing Three Ratios in Different Situations.

    Activity 1.5.6. Work in Groups.
    Consider three ratios
    \begin{gather*} 3:4, 5:8, 7:12 \end{gather*}
    1. Write the three ratios as fractions.
    2. Express each fractions as an equivalent fraction such that they have a common denominator.
    3. Compare the numerator of the equivalent fractions.
    4. Disucss and share with other groups.
    Example 1.5.9.
    Compare and find the greatest ratio among the following:
    \begin{equation*} 2:3, 5:6, 4:9 \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    Convert the ratios to fractions first.
    \begin{align*} 2:3 = \amp \frac{2}{3} \text{,} \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} 5:6 = \amp \frac{5}{6} \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} 4:9 = \amp \frac{4}{9} \end{align*}
    Then, find the LCM of the fraction which we use the denominators
    The LCM of \(3, 6, 9\) is \(18.\)
    \begin{align*} \frac{2}{3} = \amp \frac{2 \times 6}{3 \times 6} \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} \frac{5}{6} = \amp \frac{5 \times 3}{6 \times 3} \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} \frac{4}{9} = \amp \frac{4 \times 2}{9 \times 2} \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} = \amp \frac{12}{18}, \frac{15}{18}, \frac{8}{18} \end{align*}
    This means that;
    \begin{align*} 2:3 = \amp 12:18 \\ 5:6 = \amp 15:18 \\ 4:9 = \amp 8:18 \end{align*}
    Therefore, \(5:6\) is the greatest ratio.

    Subsubsection 1.5.2.4 Dividing Quantities in Given Ratios in Real Rife Situations.

    Activity 1.5.7. Work in Groups.
    Two learners some exercise books in the ratio \(4:5.\)
    1. The ratio \(4:5\) has \(4 + 5.\)
    2. The total number of parts is \(9.\)
    3. Find the number of exercise books for one part.
    4. Find the number of books recieved by each learner.
    5. Discuss and share with other groups
    \begin{align*} \amp {\textbf{Key Takeaway}} \end{align*}
    • \({\text{If a quantity is divided in the ratio} [a:b], \text{the resulting fractions of the quantity are:}}\) \({\text{for}}\) \({a;}\)
      \begin{align*} \amp {\frac{a}{(a+b)}} \end{align*}
      \({\text{and for}}\) \({b;}\)
      \begin{align*} \amp {\frac{b}{(a+b)}} \end{align*}
    • \({\text{For the ratio} [a:b:c], \text{the resulting fractions of quantities are:}}\)
      \({\text{for}}\) \({a;}\)
      \begin{align*} \amp{\frac{a}{(a+b+c)}} \end{align*}
      \({\text{for}}\) \({b;}\)
      \begin{align*} \amp {\frac{b}{(a+b+c)}} \end{align*}
      \({\text{for}}\) \({c;}\)
      \begin{align*} \amp {\frac{c}{(a+b+c)}} \end{align*}
    Example 1.5.10.
    Twenty apples were share between Cheptoo and Mwango in the ratio \(2:3\text{.}\) Find out how many apples each got?
    Solution.
    Expressing each part of the ratio as a fraction of the total, Cheptoo got \(\frac{2}{5}\) and Mwango got \(\frac{3}{5}\) of the apples.
    Mwango got \(\frac{3}{5}\) of the apples.
    Therefore, the number of apples Cheptoo got is;
    \begin{equation*} Number\, of\, apples\, Cheptoo\, got\, = \frac{2}{5} \times 20 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = \frac{40}{5} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = 8 \text{ apples} \end{equation*}
    The number of apples Mwango got is;
    \begin{equation*} Number\, of\, apples\, Mwango\, got\, = \frac{3}{5} \times 20 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = \frac{60}{5} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = 12 \text{ apples} \end{equation*}
    Example 1.5.11.
    A \(63\) hectare farm is to be shared among three sons in the ratio \(2:33:4\text{.}\) Find the size in hactares recieved by each of the sons.
    Solution.
    The total number of parts in the ratio is;
    \begin{equation*} 2 + 3 + 4 = 9 \end{equation*}
    The shares are;
    \begin{align*} \frac{2}{9} \times 63 \amp = 14\, \text{hectares} \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} \frac{3}{9} \times 63 \amp = 21\, \text{hectares} \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} \frac{4}{9} \times 63 \amp = 28\, \text{hectares} \end{align*}

    Subsubsection 1.5.2.5 Working Out Ratios in Different Situations.

    Activity 1.5.8. Work in Groups.

    Example 1.5.12.
    Six thousand litres of a chemical produsced in a factory contains susbstances \(A\) and \(B.\) If substance \(A\) in the chemical is \(2400\) litres, find the ratio of substance \(A\) to \(B\) in the chemical.
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} Total\, number\, of\, litres\, of\, substance\, A\, and\, B\, = 6000 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{Litres of substance} B = (\text{Total number of litres} - \text{Litres of substance} A) \text{litres} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = (6000 - 2400)\, litres \end{equation*}
    Ratio of \(A\) and \(B\)
    \begin{align*} \amp = 2400:3000 \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} \amp = 2:3 \end{align*}

    Subsubsection 1.5.2.6 Working Out Increase and Decrease of Quantities Using Ratio in Real Life Situations.

    Activity 1.5.9. Work in Groups..
    The number of students in a class in the first term was 54. The number increased in the ratio \(2:1\) the following term.
    1. 1 part of the ratio corresponds to \(54\text{.}\) Find the number of students for \(2\) parts..
    2. Find the number of students in the class the following term.
    3. Had the number if students decreased in the ratio \(1:2\text{,}\) find what would be the number of students.
    4. Discuss and share your results with other groups.
    \begin{align*} \amp {\text{Key Takeaway}} \end{align*}
    To increase or decrease a quantity in a given ratio, the quantity is multiplied by the corresponding fraction to the ratio.
    Example 1.5.13.
    In a certain year the population in a town was \(211400.\) In the following year the population increased in the ratio \(9:7.\) Find the population in the following year.
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} Population\, before\, was = 211400 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} Population\, increase\, ratio\, was\, = (9:7) \end{equation*}
    Population after increase is;
    \begin{equation*} 211400 \times \frac{9}{7} = \frac{9 \times 211400}{7} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = \frac{1902600}{7} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = 271800\, people \end{equation*}
    Example 1.5.14.
    The milk production in a dairy farm was \(360\) litres in a day. After one month the production reduced in the ratio \(5:9.\) Find the new dairy production per day.
    Solution.
    Fraction representing the reduction of milk production is;
    \begin{equation*} \frac{5}{9} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{Initial production} = 360 \text{ litres} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{New production} = 360 \times \frac{5}{9} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = \frac{1800}{9} \end{equation*}

    Subsection 1.5.4 Percents

    Percents are a way to express a number as a fraction of 100. The word "percent" means "per hundred." For example, 50% is the same as 50 out of 100, or 50/100. The symbol for percent is \(%.\)

    Subsubsection 1.5.4.1 Working Out Percentage Change of Given Quantities in Real Life Situations

    Activity 1.5.15. Work in Groups..
    Consider the following information
    Moraa takes five days to till a vegetable garden.
    Ruto takes ten days to till the garden.
    In your groups, discuss the following and record your findings.
    1. What fraction of the garden can Moraa till in a day?
    2. What fraction of the garden can Ruto till in a day?
    3. What fraction can they till in a day if they worked together?
    4. Share your findings with other groups.
    \begin{align*} \amp {\text{Key Takeaway}} \end{align*}
    • Increase or decrease in quantities can be expressed as percentages of the original quantities.
    • Percentage increase can be represented as;
      \begin{equation*} Percentage increase = (\frac{increase}{original}) \times 100 \% \end{equation*}
    • Percentage decrease can be represented as;
      \begin{equation*} Percentage decrease = (\frac{decrease}{original}) \times 100 \% \end{equation*}
    Example 1.5.27.
    A box cnatains \(25\) pens. If five more pens are added into the box, find the percentage increase in the number of pens in the box.
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} \text{Percentage increase} = \amp (\frac{\text{Number of pens added}}{\text{Initial number of pens}}) \times 100 \% \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} = \amp (\frac{5}{25}) \times 100 \% \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} = \amp 20 \% \end{align*}
    Example 1.5.28.
    A basket contains \(10\) mangoes. Three mangoes were removed. Find the percentage decrease in the number of mangoes in the basket.
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} \text{Percentage decrease} = \amp (\frac{\text{Number of mangoes removed}}{\text{Initial number of mangoes}}) \times 100 \% \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} = \amp (\frac{3}{10}) \times 100 \% \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} = \amp 30 \% \end{align*}
