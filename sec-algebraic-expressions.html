    Section 2.1 Algebraic expressions

    In Grade 7, we learned how to form and simplify algebraic expressions. In this section, we will explore factorization, simplifying algebraic fractions, and evaluating expressions by substitution. These concepts help us manipulate expressions efficiently, making it easier to solve equations and apply algebra in real-life situations.
    For example, when calculating costs of items, distances, or quantities, algebra simplifies complex relationships into manageable expressions.

    Subsection 2.1.1 Factorisation of algebraic expressions

    What is Factorisation?
    Factorisation is the process of expressing an algebraic expression as a product of its simpler components, making it easier to simplify and solve mathematical problems.
    It helps simplify expressions, solve equations efficiently, and understand relationships between quantities. By identifying common factors, we can rewrite algebraic terms in a more structured form, making problem-solving more efficient.
    a) Factorization by Finding Common Factors
    This method involves identifying the greatest common factor (GCF) in all terms of an expression and factoring it out.

    Example 2.1.1.

    Factorize: \(8 x + 12\)
    Solution.
    The greatest common factor (GCF) of \(8 x\) and \(12 \) is \(4 \text{.}\)
    \(4 \) divides \(8 x\) and \(12 \) to get;
    \begin{equation*} 4(2 x + 3) \end{equation*}
    b) Factorization of Algebraic Expressions by Grouping
    This technique is useful when an expression has four terms that can be grouped into pairs, each having a common factor.

    Example 2.1.2.

    Factorize \(ax + ay + bx + by\)
    Solution.
    Group the terms (like terms) which look similar
    \begin{equation*} (ax + ay) + (bx + by) \end{equation*}
    Factor out common terms in each group:
    \begin{equation*} a(x + y) + b(x + y) \end{equation*}
    Factor out the common factor \((x + y)\)
    \begin{equation*} (x + y)(a + b) \end{equation*}
    New word; like terms
    Like terms are terms that have the same variable and the same power. They can be added or subtracted because they belong to the same group.

    If there are \(5 \) baskets, write an algebraic expression for the total number of mangoes and bananas.

    \begin{equation*} 5 \times a = 5 a \end{equation*}
    Bananas in \(5 \) baskets
    \begin{equation*} 5 \times b = 5 b \end{equation*}
    Total number of fruits:
    \begin{equation*} 5 a + 5 b \end{equation*}
    Factorizing with \(5 \) we get;
    \begin{equation*} 5( a + b) \end{equation*}

    Subsection 2.1.2 Simplifying algebraic fractions

    Algebraic fractions are fractions that have algebraic expressions in their numerator, denominator or with them.
    Factorization method of simplification
    Have you ever shared something equally among friends, like sharing food? When working with algebraic fractions, we apply similar ideas by finding common terms and simplifying them.

    Activity 2.1.2.

    Group Activity: Work in groups
    What you need: A book or a paper and a pen
    A mechanic workshop has two workers, Amani and Baraka. Amani completes \(\frac {1}{3}\) of a car repair job in an hour, while Baraka completes \(\frac {1}{4}\) of the same job in an hour.
    Taking any given letter to represent the total amount of work, find:
    • How much work do they complete together in one hour?
    • How do we express this as a simplified algebraic fraction?
    Key Takeaway
    We can simplify algebraic fractions the same way we simplify numerical fractions
    • Find the Least Common Multiple (LCM)
    • Rewrite fractions with the same denominator
    • Combine like terms, then simplify

    Example 2.1.4.

    Simplify the algebraic fraction:
    \begin{equation*} \frac {x}{3} + \frac {2x}{4} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    First, we find the LCM of \(3\) and \(4\text{,}\) which is \(12\)
    Then,we rewrite each fraction,
    \begin{equation*} \frac {x}{3} + \frac {2x}{4} = \frac {4(x) + 3(2x)}{12} \end{equation*}
    Then we add the numerators,
    \begin{equation*} = \frac {4x + 6x}{12} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \frac {10x}{12} \end{equation*}
    Now, we simplify;
    \begin{equation*} \frac {5x}{6} \end{equation*}
    Sometimes we simplify fractions with \(like\) \(terms\) and sometimes \(unlike\) \(terms.\)

    Example 2.1.5.

    Simplify the algebraic expression, \(\frac {3}{4} a + \frac {5}{6} b\)
    Solution.
    Find the LCM of the denominators
    The denominators are \(4\) and \(6\text{,}\) getting \(12\)
    Rewrite each fraction with denominator \(12\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac {3}{4} a + \frac {5}{6} b = \frac {9a + 10b}{12} \end{equation*}
    Since the terms are unlike (one has \('a'\) and the other has \('b'\)), we cannot combine them, therefore having;
    \begin{equation*} \frac {9a + 10b}{12} \end{equation*}

    Example 2.1.6.

    Simplify the algebraic fraction:
    \begin{equation*} \frac {3x + 9}{6x + 18} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    Here we need to factor out the common factors in the numerator and denominator
    The numerator \(3x + 9 \) has a common factor of \(3 \text{,}\) So:
    \begin{equation*} 3x + 9 = 3( x + 3) \end{equation*}
    The denomenator \(6x + 18 \) has a common factor of \(6 \text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} 6x + 18 = 6 ( x + 3) \end{equation*}
    Rewriting the fraction in factored forms:
    \begin{equation*} \frac {3 ( x + 3)}{6 ( x + 3)} \end{equation*}
    Now the common factor \(( x + 3)\) cancels out;
    \begin{equation*} \frac {3 ( x + 3)}{6 ( x + 3)} \end{equation*}
    getting,
    \begin{equation*} \frac {3}{6} = \frac {1}{2} \end{equation*}

    Subsection 2.1.3 Evaluating algebraic expressions by substitution

    We often deal with formulas and expressions on our day-to-day calculations. For example, calculating the total cost of items, determining the speed of a moving object, or finding the area of a piece of land. These formulas use letters (variables) to represent unknown values. In this section, we will learn how to evaluate algebraic expressions by replacing variables with given numbers.

    Example 2.1.7.

    \(Naree\) Bus company charges \(60 m + 150\) shillings for a trip to town, where \(m\) is the number of kilometres travelled. Find the total cost for a \(7 km\) trip.
    Solution.
    Here we are given \(m\) = \(7\)
    Therefore,
    \begin{equation*} 60 m + 150 = 60 (7) + 150 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = 420 + 150 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = 570 shillings \end{equation*}

    Example 2.1.8.

    Evaluate \(3a + 2b - c\)
    where \(a\) = 5, \(b\) = 3 and \(c\) = 7
    Solution.
    To solve the question, we need to substitute the values of \(a\text{,}\) \(b\) and \(c\) into the expression;
    \(a\) = 5, \(b\) = 3 and \(c\) = 7;
    So
    \begin{equation*} 3a + 2b - c \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = 3(5) + 2(3) - (7) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = 15 + 6 - 7 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = 14 \end{equation*}
    Key Takeaway
    • Variables help represent unknown values and changing quantities. How can these tools help in real-life algebra problems?
    • Substituting values makes it easier to evaluate expressions. Such as using formulas in recipes, calculating transport expenses e.t.c.
    • Application of algebraic expressions is important in budgeting, farming measurements, business profit calculations and savings planning.
